When you retire, $70,000/year feels like plenty. Twenty-five years later, at 2.5% annual inflation, that same lifestyle costs $129,600/year. Your money needs to work harder than just not losing — it needs to grow.

This is the inflation problem in retirement.

## The Math of Purchasing Power Erosion

At 2.5% annual inflation:
- $1,000 today = $778 in 10 years
- $1,000 today = $605 in 20 years
- $1,000 today = $470 in 25 years

A retiree with $70,000/year in fixed income at 65 is effectively receiving $32,900/year in today's dollars at 90. Everything that isn't inflation-indexed loses this fight automatically.

## What Is (and Isn't) Inflation-Protected

**Inflation-protected:**
- CPP: Indexed to CPI every January
- OAS: Indexed to CPI every January (quarterly adjustments)
- Defined benefit pensions: Many include CPI indexing, but not all — check your plan

**Not inflation-protected:**
- Fixed annuities
- GIC income
- Bond interest income
- RRIF/RRSP withdrawals of fixed amounts
- Most company pensions outside the public sector

The distinction matters enormously over a 25-year retirement.

## Canada's Recent Inflation Experience

The 2021–2023 inflation surge reminded Canadians that inflation isn't always 2%. Retirees on fixed incomes saw their purchasing power decline sharply in 18 months.

CPP and OAS recipients benefited from the CPI indexing — their payments increased significantly in 2022 and 2023. Annuity holders and those with fixed pension income had no such adjustment.

## Investment Strategies That Hedge Inflation

### Equities

Over long periods, equity investments have outpaced inflation — the "equity risk premium." Canadian and international stocks have historically returned 7–10% nominally and 4–7% in real terms.

For retirees, maintaining a meaningful equity allocation (40–60%) rather than shifting entirely to bonds provides an inflation hedge. The risk is volatility — especially in the early retirement sequence-of-returns window.

### Real Return Bonds

The Government of Canada issues Real Return Bonds (RRBs) — bonds that adjust their principal with CPI. The real yield is low (often negative in recent years), but the inflation protection is guaranteed.

RRBs are available in registered accounts. For conservative retirees who want to guarantee inflation protection, they're worth considering for a portion of fixed income holdings.

### REITs

Real Estate Investment Trusts tend to benefit from inflation because property values and rents typically rise with or above inflation. Canadian REIT ETFs (XRE, ZRE) provide diversified real estate exposure within a portfolio.

### TIPs (US Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities)

Available in RRSP/TFSA through US-listed ETFs. Similar to Canadian RRBs but larger market. Useful for US dollar inflation exposure.

### Infrastructure and Utilities

Regulated utilities often have revenues tied to inflation through regulatory agreements. Infrastructure assets (pipelines, toll roads) have similar characteristics.

## CPP and OAS as Your Inflation Foundation

The most powerful inflation hedge available to most Canadians isn't a financial instrument — it's maximizing CPP and OAS.

Deferring CPP and OAS to 70 increases your inflation-indexed lifetime income permanently. Every additional dollar of CPP or OAS is indexed to CPI forever. Every additional dollar from a GIC or annuity is not.

A retiree who defers both CPP and OAS to 70 receives ~$28,000/year in indexed income. A retiree who takes both at 65 receives ~$19,000/year indexed. The $9,000 difference is inflation-adjusted and compounds — at age 85, the difference in real terms is even larger.

## Practical Inflation Planning

When building your retirement plan, use a realistic inflation assumption:
- **2.5%** is the Bank of Canada's midpoint target
- **3.0%** is more conservative and worth testing as a scenario
- Running your plan at both 2.5% and 3.5% inflation shows the range of outcomes

For spending, assume costs rise at inflation in general but healthcare costs at 1–2% above inflation (historical healthcare inflation in Canada has consistently exceeded general CPI).

## The Bottom Line

Inflation is not optional. Every retirement plan must account for it. The primary defenses are:
1. Inflation-indexed income (CPP, OAS — maximize both)
2. Equity exposure for real growth
3. Flexible spending that can adjust with purchasing power
4. Avoiding large allocations to fixed-rate instruments you can't rebalance
