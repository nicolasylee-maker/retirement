# CPP Enhancement Explained: How the Changes Affect Your Retirement

If you've noticed your CPP deductions increasing since 2019, that's by design. The federal government launched a multi-year CPP enhancement to significantly boost retirement benefits for future retirees. Here's what it means for you.

## Why CPP Was Enhanced

The original CPP was designed to replace 25% of pre-retirement earnings (up to the Year's Maximum Pensionable Earnings, or YMPE). The government determined this wasn't enough — especially as defined benefit pensions disappeared from the private sector.

The enhancement, fully phased in by 2025, raises the replacement rate to **33.33%** of covered earnings, and extends coverage to a higher earnings ceiling.

## Two Phases of Enhancement

### Phase 1 (2019–2023): Higher Contribution Rate

Over five years, employee and employer CPP contribution rates increased from 4.95% to 5.95% of pensionable earnings. Self-employed individuals pay both sides (11.9%).

This phase increased the income replacement rate from 25% to 33.33%.

### Phase 2 (2024–2025): Second Earnings Ceiling

A new "CPP2" layer was added. Earnings between the YMPE ($71,300 in 2025) and a new Year's Additional Maximum Pensionable Earnings (YAMPE, $81,900 in 2025) are now subject to additional contributions of 4% (employee) and 4% (employer).

This second tier builds a separate, additional benefit on top of the base CPP.

## What the Enhanced CPP Pays

The enhancement affects **only the years you contributed at the higher rate**. If you worked from 2019–2030 and then retired, roughly 11 of your peak years will include enhanced contributions. The boost is proportional to how many years you contributed under the enhanced rules.

**For workers who contributed under the full enhancement for their entire career** (i.e., started working around 2019 or later and retire around 2065): the maximum CPP at 65 will eventually be around **$2,000–$2,200/month** in today's dollars, up from the current ~$1,364 maximum.

**For workers already in their 50s or 60s in 2019:** the benefit boost is smaller — you only receive enhanced amounts for the years 2019 onward. If you retired in 2025 having contributed from 2019–2025 at enhanced rates, your benefit is maybe 5–8% higher than it would have been without enhancement.

## CPP2: The Separate Second Tier

The CPP2 contributions on earnings from YMPE to YAMPE are tracked separately. If you contributed to CPP2 in 2024 and 2025, you'll receive a small additional benefit on top of your regular CPP.

In 2025, the maximum CPP2 benefit adds about $40–$60/month for workers who contributed at the new ceiling — very small because the program just started.

Over decades, CPP2 will become more significant for higher earners.

## How to See Your Projected CPP Amount

Log into **My Service Canada Account** at canada.ca and navigate to CPP Statement of Contributions. This shows:

- Your contribution history year by year
- Your projected CPP benefit at 60, 65, and 70

The "at 65" estimate assumes you stop contributing now and is the most relevant for planning. The "at 70" estimate includes the 0.7%/month increase for deferral.

## What Enhanced CPP Means for Your RRSP Strategy

Higher future CPP income changes the RRSP equation:

**If you expect higher CPP:** You might need slightly less in RRSP savings, but the difference is often smaller than people think. Enhanced CPP adds perhaps $200–$400/month for mid-career workers, which is $2,400–$4,800/year — meaningful but not a reason to slash RRSP contributions.

**The OAS clawback consideration:** Higher CPP income in retirement pushes your total income closer to the OAS clawback threshold ($93,454 in 2025). For high earners, enhanced CPP could increase OAS recovery tax slightly. This is worth modeling if you expect retirement income above $85,000/year.

**Pension splitting:** CPP is not eligible for pension income splitting the way RRIF withdrawals or defined benefit pensions are. Each spouse gets their own CPP. Enhanced CPP benefits each earner individually.

## Self-Employed Canadians: A Special Burden

Self-employed individuals pay both the employee and employer share of CPP — now 11.9% on base earnings plus 8% on CPP2 earnings. This can be a substantial amount.

The good news: you deduct the **employer portion** as a business expense, and claim the employee portion as a non-refundable tax credit. But the cash flow impact is real: a self-employed person earning $81,900 in 2025 pays roughly $8,900 in CPP contributions total.

For the self-employed, the enhanced CPP is effectively forced retirement savings. Those who might not otherwise save disciplined amounts will benefit significantly when CPP payments begin.

## Does the Enhanced CPP Change When You Should Take CPP?

Not fundamentally. The optimal CPP timing strategy (delay to 70 if you're in good health) remains the same. Delaying from 65 to 70 still increases your payment by 42% — and that higher payment is calculated on whatever your entitlement is, including the enhancement.

If anything, enhanced CPP at 70 is even more valuable to defer, since a higher base amount gets the 42% deferral bonus applied.

## Summary

| Aspect | Detail |
|---|---|
| Phase 1 enhancement | 2019–2023, rate raised to 5.95% |
| Phase 2 (CPP2) | 2024–2025, earnings up to YAMPE |
| Full income replacement | 33.33% (up from 25%) |
| Full benefit (future workers) | ~$2,000–$2,200/month |
| Impact for near-retirees | 5–10% benefit increase for 2019–2025 contributions |

The CPP enhancement is a meaningful improvement to Canada's retirement system. It won't solve retirement insecurity alone, but it reduces the savings burden for younger workers and provides a more reliable income floor for everyone.
