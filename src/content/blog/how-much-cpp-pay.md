The CPP maximum pension in 2025 is $1,364.60/month. But the average Canadian receives $780/month — barely half the maximum. The difference is your actual contribution history, and most people have no idea what their real number is until they look.

Here's how to find your actual CPP entitlement and what affects it.

## How CPP Benefits Are Calculated

CPP is based on the **average of your best years of earnings** between 18 and 65, subject to each year's YMPE (Yearly Maximum Pensionable Earnings).

The 2025 YMPE is $71,300. If you earned more than this, only $71,300 counts toward CPP. If you earned less, only your actual earnings count.

There's a 17% dropout provision: the lowest 17% of your earning years are dropped from the calculation. This helps people who had low-income years early in life or took time off.

You also need to have contributed at least one valid year to receive any benefit.

## The CPP Enhancement (Post-2019)

Since 2019, CPP has been enhanced. Contributions are higher (both employee and employer), and the eventual benefit will be higher for those who contributed during the enhancement years.

If you're currently working and contributing, you're building enhanced CPP on top of the base. The enhancement will phase in fully over 40 years — meaning younger workers will eventually receive significantly more than today's maximums.

## How to Find Your Actual Entitlement

**Step 1:** Log into My Service Canada Account at canada.ca/en/employment-social-development/services/my-account.html

**Step 2:** Go to Canada Pension Plan → Statement of Contributions

**Step 3:** Look at your contribution history year by year. Low-income years or years out of the workforce show up clearly.

**Step 4:** Use the CPP retirement pension estimator (also on My Service Canada) to see a projected benefit at different start ages.

This is the only way to get a personalized number. Generic calculators that assume you've maxed out contributions for 35 years are often 20–40% too high for the average person.

## Factors That Reduce Your CPP

**Years out of the workforce:** Every year of zero or low earnings reduces your average, even after the dropout provision. Common causes: parental leave, caregiving, school, layoff.

**Lower-than-maximum earnings:** If you earned $50,000 in years when the YMPE was $70,000, those years contribute proportionally less.

**Self-employment gaps:** Self-employed Canadians pay both the employee and employer portion of CPP contributions — 2× the rate of employees. Under-contributing or not contributing as self-employed significantly reduces benefits.

**Early withdrawal:** Taking CPP before 65 reduces your benefit permanently by 7.2% per year under 65.

## Factors That Increase Your CPP

**Working longer:** Contributing for more years with higher earnings raises your average.

**High earnings:** Maximizing pensionable earnings up to the YMPE each year builds toward the maximum benefit.

**Delaying to 70:** The maximum increase is 42% by deferring from 65 to 70.

**Post-retirement benefit:** If you continue working after starting CPP (before 70), you continue contributing and earn a small additional post-retirement benefit each year.

## What the Numbers Mean for Planning

Most Canadians should use a realistic CPP estimate of $800–$1,100/month, not the $1,364 maximum, unless their Service Canada account confirms a higher projection.

Planning with $1,364 when your actual entitlement is $850 means you're underestimating your income shortfall by $514/month ($6,168/year). At a 4% withdrawal rate, that's a $154,200 gap in required savings.

Always use your actual Service Canada projection number.
