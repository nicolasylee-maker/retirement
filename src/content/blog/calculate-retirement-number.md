"What's my retirement number?" This is the first question people ask when they start thinking seriously about retirement. The honest answer: there isn't one number — there's a range, and the range depends on assumptions that matter enormously.

Here's how to calculate it correctly.

## Step 1: Determine Your Annual Spending in Retirement

This is the most important input and the most commonly guessed. Don't guess. Do one of:

- Track your spending for 3 months and annualize
- Look at your last 3 years of bank and credit card statements
- Use a retirement spending worksheet

Adjust for what changes:
- **Remove:** Mortgage (if paid off), work-related expenses, saving contributions, payroll taxes
- **Add:** Healthcare insurance, travel/leisure, home maintenance, hobbies

A common finding: retirement spending is 70–90% of pre-retirement spending in the early years, declining to 60–70% in later years.

**For this example, we'll use $70,000/year.**

## Step 2: Identify Your Guaranteed Income

Every dollar of guaranteed lifetime income reduces the savings you need.

**CPP:** Get your actual estimate from My Service Canada. Let's say $950/month = $11,400/year.

**OAS:** Full OAS at 65 = $8,724/year. Both you and your spouse if applicable.

**Defined benefit pension:** Get the projected amount from your plan.

**For this single-person example:** CPP + OAS = $20,124/year.

## Step 3: Calculate Your Annual Gap

Annual gap = Spending – Guaranteed income

$70,000 – $20,124 = **$49,876/year gap**

This is the amount your savings must produce every year.

## Step 4: Apply the Withdrawal Rate

The safe withdrawal rate (SWR) converts an annual income need into a lump-sum savings requirement.

**Conservative (3.5% SWR):** $49,876 ÷ 0.035 = **$1,425,000**

**Moderate (4.0% SWR):** $49,876 ÷ 0.04 = **$1,247,000**

**Optimistic (4.5% SWR):** $49,876 ÷ 0.045 = **$1,108,000**

Your range is $1.1M–$1.4M. The wide range shows why the withdrawal rate assumption matters.

## Step 5: Adjust for Taxes

The above assumes after-tax spending. Your RRSP/RRIF withdrawals are taxable.

If you need $49,876 after tax and you're in a 28% marginal bracket, you need to gross up the RRIF withdrawal:

$49,876 ÷ (1 – 0.28) = **$69,272 gross RRIF withdrawal**

But TFSA withdrawals aren't taxed. If half the gap comes from TFSA and half from RRIF:
- TFSA contribution: $24,938 (no gross-up)
- RRIF gross-up: $24,938 ÷ 0.72 = $34,636
- Total gross withdrawal needed: $59,574

The tax adjustment reduces the apparent savings requirement but means you need to be in the right accounts. This is why account structure (RRSP vs. TFSA mix) affects your actual retirement number.

## Step 6: Adjust for Time Horizon

The 4% rule assumes a 30-year retirement (age 65–95). Adjust:

- **Retire at 55:** Use 3.5% SWR for 40-year horizon
- **Retire at 65:** 4% is standard
- **Retire at 70:** 4.5–5% may be defensible for 25-year horizon

## Step 7: Build Your Range

Rather than a single number, present yourself with three scenarios:

| Scenario | Assumptions | Required Savings |
|----------|------------|-----------------|
| Lean (optimistic) | $65K spending, defer CPP/OAS to 70, 4.5% SWR | ~$900,000 |
| Base case | $70K spending, take CPP/OAS at 65, 4% SWR | ~$1,250,000 |
| Conservative | $80K spending, CPP/OAS at 65, 3.5% SWR | ~$1,700,000 |

Your actual number is probably between the Lean and Conservative scenarios. Planning to the Conservative number gives you the best protection against living longer, spending more, or returns coming in low.

## The Interactive Version

The framework above gives you a calculation, but real retirement planning involves year-by-year projections — because CPP starts at a different time than OAS, RRIF minimums increase over time, and spending changes with age.

A flat annual-gap calculation overestimates your needs in years when CPP/OAS are high relative to spending, and underestimates in the pre-government-income years.
