Retiring at 50 or 55 is possible in Canada. But it's categorically different from retiring at 65. The math changes, the rules change, and the risks are higher.

Here's what you actually need to know.

## The Early Retirement Gap Problem

At 65, you have CPP, OAS, and potentially RRIF income all available simultaneously. At 55, none of these exist yet.

**CPP minimum age:** 60. And if you take it at 60, it's reduced by 36% permanently.

**OAS minimum age:** 65. Full stop — you can't access it earlier.

**RRSP/RRIF:** You can withdraw anytime, but early heavy withdrawals at 55–65 increase the risk of depleting your account before mandatory conversion at 71.

**TFSA:** No restrictions. You can withdraw at any time, at any age, for any reason.

The result: early retirees must self-fund 100% of their income from personal savings for 5–15 years, with no government income floor.

## What Early Retirement Actually Costs

The extra cost isn't just 10 more years of spending. It compounds:

1. **10 more years of living expenses** — let's say $60,000/year = $600,000 more needed (not inflation-adjusted)
2. **10 fewer years of portfolio growth** — $500,000 invested for 10 extra years at 6% = $396,000 more
3. **Reduced CPP** — 10 fewer contribution years and early start penalty reduces CPP by $300–$600/month vs. retiring at 65
4. **No employer benefits** — health, dental, and disability insurance must be privately funded

The combined effect: retiring at 55 vs. 65 may require 60–80% more in total savings.

## The TFSA Bridge Strategy

The most common early retirement structure for Canadians:

1. Build a large TFSA (goal: $300,000–$500,000+)
2. Retire at 55–60
3. Draw from TFSA from retirement to 65, minimizing taxable income
4. At 60, take CPP if needed (or defer if TFSA can bridge to 65+)
5. At 65, begin OAS
6. At 71, convert RRSP to RRIF

Why TFSA first? TFSA withdrawals are tax-free and don't affect income-tested benefits. Living on TFSA income while deferring RRSP withdrawals keeps your income low, which:
- Reduces income tax in your bridge years
- Preserves GIS eligibility (if applicable)
- Doesn't count toward OAS clawback threshold
- Allows CPP deferral without income pressure

## The "Rule of 25" for Early Retirement

A rough planning heuristic for early retirement: your portfolio must be at least 25× your annual gap (spending minus guaranteed income).

But in Canada, your guaranteed income is $0 until at least 60. For early retirees, the full spending amount must come from portfolio until CPP/OAS kicks in.

For $60,000/year spending from 55–65 with zero government income:
- Pre-CPP/OAS savings needed: 25 × $60,000 = $1,500,000 (to sustain indefinitely)
- Or calculate the bridge fund separately: $60,000 × 10 years = $600,000 for the bridge, plus post-65 savings for the gap after government income begins

## Health Insurance: The Hidden Cost

In Canada, provincial health insurance covers most essential medical care — but not:
- Dental
- Vision
- Prescription drugs (in most provinces)
- Extended physiotherapy, chiropractic, etc.
- Long-term care

Employer benefits typically cover these. Early retirees lose that coverage.

Private extended health insurance for a healthy 55-year-old couple: $400–$800/month depending on coverage. Over 10 years of early retirement bridge: $48,000–$96,000 in insurance costs alone.

## The CPP Penalty: Is It Worth Taking Early?

For early retirees who need income from 60 onward:
- CPP at 60: $640/month (using $1,000 base example)
- CPP at 65: $1,000/month
- CPP at 70: $1,420/month

If your TFSA can fund the gap from 60 to 65, defer CPP to 65 or later. Every year of deferral adds 7.2–8.4% to your lifetime payment.

If you genuinely need CPP income at 60, take it — but understand the permanent reduction.

## Sequence of Returns Risk Is Higher

Early retirees face a longer sequence-of-returns window. A market crash at 56 — while withdrawing with no government income — is more damaging than the same crash at 66 when CPP and OAS are covering half your expenses.

A larger cash/short-term bond buffer (3–5 years of living expenses rather than 1–2) is appropriate for early retirees.
