Your RRSP must be converted to a Registered Retirement Income Fund (RRIF) by December 31 of the year you turn 71. From then on, you must withdraw a minimum amount every year — taxed as income, whether you need the money or not.

Understanding RRIF minimums is essential for anyone with a significant RRSP.

## RRIF Minimum Withdrawal Rates (2025)

The minimum withdrawal is calculated as a percentage of your RRIF's fair market value at January 1 each year.

| Age | Minimum Rate |
|-----|-------------|
| 71 | 5.28% |
| 72 | 5.40% |
| 73 | 5.53% |
| 74 | 5.67% |
| 75 | 5.82% |
| 76 | 5.98% |
| 77 | 6.17% |
| 78 | 6.36% |
| 79 | 6.58% |
| 80 | 6.82% |
| 85 | 8.51% |
| 90 | 11.92% |
| 95+ | 20.00% |

## The Compounding Problem

At 72, a 5.40% minimum withdrawal from a $500,000 RRIF is $27,000. That's manageable.

By 82, the minimum rate is 7.38%. If your RRIF has grown to $700,000 (after withdrawals and investment returns), you're forced to take $51,660. This stacks on top of CPP, OAS, and any other income.

For many Canadians, RRIF minimums in their late 70s and 80s push them into tax brackets higher than their working years. The mandatory nature of the withdrawal is what makes it a tax planning problem.

## How RRIF Minimums Are Calculated

**Step 1:** Note your RRIF balance on January 1 (or the value at date of conversion if converting in that year).
**Step 2:** Find your age-based percentage from the table above.
**Step 3:** Multiply balance × percentage = minimum withdrawal.

You can withdraw more than the minimum at any time. You cannot withdraw less.

**Spousal age option:** If your spouse is younger, you can elect to base minimum withdrawals on their age, resulting in lower minimums. This is a one-time election made at RRIF setup and cannot be reversed.

## The Tax Impact

RRIF withdrawals are reported on a T4RIF slip and fully included in income. They're eligible for the pension income amount credit ($2,000 max), but otherwise treated like employment income.

**Withholding taxes apply** to withdrawals above the minimum:
- $0–$5,000 above minimum: 10% withholding
- $5,001–$15,000 above minimum: 20% withholding
- $15,001+: 30% withholding

These are just withholding amounts — your actual tax rate is determined at filing.

## Strategies to Manage RRIF Tax

**Convert later:** You must convert by 71, but you can convert earlier. Some people convert at 65 to start drawing taxable income in low-bracket years while delaying CPP and OAS.

**Melt down before conversion:** The RRSP meltdown (drawing down your RRSP from ages 60–70) reduces the RRIF balance and therefore future minimums.

**Use the spousal age option:** If your spouse is younger, electing their age as the base for minimums meaningfully reduces forced income.

**Pension income splitting:** At 65+, RRIF withdrawals are eligible pension income for splitting purposes. Split up to 50% with your spouse to equalize tax brackets.

**RRIF to TFSA:** If you don't need all RRIF withdrawals for living expenses, the after-tax proceeds can be transferred to your TFSA (subject to contribution room). This converts future taxable growth to tax-free growth.

## What Happens to RRIF Funds on Death

On death, the RRIF balance passes to the named beneficiary:

**Surviving spouse named:** The RRIF rolls over tax-free to the spouse's own RRIF. No tax event on transfer.

**Other beneficiary or estate:** The full RRIF balance is included in the deceased's final tax return as income. This can result in a very large tax bill in the year of death.

Name your spouse as beneficiary on all RRIF accounts. If you have no spouse, consider RRIF drawdown or annuity conversion to reduce the estate tax hit.
