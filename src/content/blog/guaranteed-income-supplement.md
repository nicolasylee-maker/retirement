The Guaranteed Income Supplement is one of Canada's most valuable but least understood senior benefits. It pays up to $1,057/month tax-free — on top of OAS — to seniors with low incomes.

Here's who qualifies, how much you can get, and the critical trap that causes some low-income seniors to inadvertently lose most of it.

## What Is GIS?

GIS is a non-taxable monthly payment to OAS recipients whose income is below the threshold. It's designed to supplement the incomes of the lowest-income Canadian seniors.

Unlike OAS and CPP, GIS is not indexed annually — it's adjusted quarterly based on CPI and is fully income-tested.

## 2025 GIS Maximum Amounts

| Situation | Monthly Maximum | Annual Maximum |
|-----------|----------------|---------------|
| Single, widowed, or divorced | $1,057.01 | $12,684 |
| Couple, both on OAS | $636.26 each | $15,270 combined |
| Couple, spouse on Allowance | $636.26 + Allowance | varies |

Combined with OAS, a single qualifying senior can receive up to **$1,784.68/month** in 2025 — about $21,416/year. This is Canada's effective income floor for seniors.

## Who Qualifies?

You must:
1. Receive OAS (age 65+ with 10+ years of Canadian residency)
2. Have low income — below the threshold for your situation
3. Reside in Canada

**2025 income thresholds for full GIS:**
- Single: $0 other income (GIS reduces from maximum as income rises)
- Maximum income to receive any GIS (single): ~$22,056/year

GIS is reduced by **50 cents for every dollar of other income** above zero (excluding OAS itself and a few specific exclusions).

## The Critical RRSP/RRIF Trap

This is the most important planning point for low-income seniors with any RRSP savings:

**RRSP/RRIF withdrawals count as income for GIS purposes.** Every $1,000 you withdraw from an RRSP or RRIF reduces your GIS by $500.

Combined with income tax on the withdrawal, the effective marginal rate for a GIS-eligible senior making RRSP withdrawals can exceed **70%**.

**Example:** A senior receiving maximum GIS withdraws $5,000 from their RRSP to replace the furnace.
- GIS reduction: $2,500/year
- Income tax at ~20% marginal rate: ~$1,000
- Net cost of $5,000 withdrawal: $3,500 out of pocket, $3,500 to government
- Effective tax rate: 70%

The implication: if you're at or near GIS eligibility, RRSP withdrawals must be timed extremely carefully. Large withdrawals in one year temporarily eliminate GIS for the following year (GIS is recalculated annually based on prior year income).

## TFSA Withdrawals Are GIS-Safe

TFSA withdrawals are excluded from income calculations entirely. For low-income seniors with both RRSP/RRIF savings and TFSA savings, exhausting the TFSA completely before touching the RRSP — or converting RRSP to TFSA during low-income years before 65 — can preserve GIS eligibility.

## How to Apply

GIS is not automatic. You must apply after starting OAS.

**If you file a tax return:** Service Canada automatically assesses your GIS eligibility after each tax filing. You'll receive a letter if you qualify.

**If you don't file taxes:** You must apply manually with Form ISP-3550 each year. Low-income seniors who don't file taxes sometimes miss years of GIS they were entitled to.

**Retroactive payments:** GIS can be paid retroactively for up to 11 months of missed payments.

## The Allowance Program

If you're 60–64 and your spouse receives OAS + GIS, you may qualify for the **Allowance** — a similar benefit of up to $1,381.90/month (2025). It stops when you turn 65 and begin your own OAS and GIS.

There's also the **Allowance for the Survivor** for those 60–64 who have lost a spouse.
