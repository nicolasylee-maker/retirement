# How Much Should You Have Saved by Age 60 in Canada?

Age 60 is a critical milestone — you're typically 2–7 years from retirement, close enough to see it clearly but still with time to course-correct. The question "am I on track?" is both urgent and answerable.

## The Honest Answer: It Depends

Common rules of thumb (like "10x your salary by retirement") are American-centric and ignore Canada's public pension system. CPP and OAS together can replace a significant portion of pre-retirement income, which means Canadians typically need less in personal savings than Americans.

Here's a framework built for Canadian reality.

## Step 1: Estimate Your CPP and OAS

**CPP at 65:** The average CPP payment in 2025 is about $815/month. Maximum is $1,364/month. If you've worked 35+ years at average or above-average earnings, expect $900–$1,200/month.

**OAS at 65:** Everyone who's lived in Canada for 40+ years gets the full $727/month (2025). Takes effect at 65 (or 70 if deferred for 36% more).

Together, CPP + OAS = roughly $1,600–$2,000/month for most Canadians, or $19,200–$24,000/year.

This is your baseline. Your personal savings need to fill the gap between this and your target retirement income.

## Step 2: Define Your Target Income

Most Canadians spend 60–80% of pre-retirement income in retirement. Healthcare goes up; commuting, work clothes, and saving itself go down.

**Common targets by lifestyle:**

| Lifestyle | Annual Spending |
|---|---|
| Frugal (small town, paid-off home) | $35,000–$45,000 |
| Moderate (average city, some travel) | $50,000–$65,000 |
| Comfortable (urban, active travel) | $70,000–$90,000 |
| Affluent | $90,000+ |

## Step 3: Calculate the Gap

Your savings must bridge the gap between CPP + OAS and your target spending.

**Example — Moderate lifestyle, $60,000/year target:**
- CPP + OAS: $22,000/year
- Gap to fill from savings: $38,000/year
- Years in retirement: 25 (age 65–90)
- Required savings (at 4% withdrawal rate): $38,000 ÷ 0.04 = **$950,000**

**Example — Frugal, $40,000/year target:**
- CPP + OAS: $20,000/year
- Gap: $20,000/year
- Required savings: $20,000 ÷ 0.04 = **$500,000**

## Where You Should Be at 60

If you're planning to retire at 65 and you have 5 more years of saving and compounding, working backwards:

Assuming 5% annual growth on your existing savings and you're saving an additional $15,000–$25,000/year:

| Target at 65 | Approximate Balance Needed at 60 |
|---|---|
| $500,000 | ~$300,000–$350,000 |
| $750,000 | ~$480,000–$530,000 |
| $1,000,000 | ~$650,000–$720,000 |
| $1,500,000 | ~$1,000,000–$1,100,000 |

*Assumes 5% average return and $15,000–$20,000/year in continued contributions.*

## The Median Reality

Statistics Canada data shows the median net worth of Canadians aged 55–64 is approximately $690,000 — but this includes home equity, which isn't spendable unless you sell or take a reverse mortgage.

Median **financial assets** (RRSP, TFSA, non-registered) for this age group is closer to $150,000–$250,000. Most Canadians are relying on their home equity plus CPP/OAS more than they realize.

## If You're Behind at 60: Five Levers

**1. Work longer.** Every year you delay retirement does three things simultaneously: you accumulate more savings, you get one more year of CPP contributions (increasing your benefit), and you shorten the period your savings need to last.

**2. Defer CPP and OAS.** Delaying CPP past 65 increases your payment by 8.4% per year (up to 70). If you can cover ages 65–70 from savings, the higher CPP replaces more income for life. OAS deferred to 70 pays 36% more.

**3. Downsize your home.** If you own a $900,000 home and move to a $500,000 condo, you free $400,000 in tax-free capital (principal residence exemption). This can close a large savings gap instantly.

**4. Maximize RRSP and TFSA in your final working years.** Your 60s are often peak earning years with fewer expenses (kids moved out, mortgage paid). Aggressive saving in these years has an outsized impact.

**5. Reduce retirement income target.** Review discretionary spending plans. Many retirees find their actual spending is 15–20% lower than projected because travel slows, spending habits change, and paid-off mortgages reduce housing costs dramatically.

## Don't Forget Defined Benefit Pensions

If you have a DB pension — government, teacher, healthcare, or some corporate plans — treat each $1,000/month of pension income as equivalent to roughly $300,000 in savings (at a 4% withdrawal rate). A teacher retiring with $3,000/month pension effectively has the equivalent of $900,000 in savings.

DB pensions dramatically reduce how much you need in personal savings.

## Run Your Own Numbers

The benchmarks above are starting points. Your actual number depends on:

- Your exact CPP entitlement (check My Service Canada Account)
- Whether you have a pension
- Whether your home is paid off
- Your province (affects taxes significantly)
- Your planned retirement age
- Your spending habits
