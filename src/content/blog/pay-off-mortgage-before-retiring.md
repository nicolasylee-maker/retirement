Should you prioritize paying off your mortgage or maximizing retirement savings? It's one of the most common questions in Canadian personal finance — and the right answer depends on the math and your psychology.

## The Math Framework

The core question: Is your mortgage interest rate higher or lower than your expected after-tax investment return?

**If mortgage rate > investment return:** Pay off the mortgage. Every dollar paid reduces a guaranteed cost, while investment returns are uncertain.

**If investment return > mortgage rate:** Invest. The higher expected return on investments outweighs the guaranteed return from mortgage paydown.

**Example (2025 rates):**
- Mortgage rate: 5.5%
- Expected RRSP investment return: 6–7%
- Expected TFSA investment return: 6–7%

At 5.5% vs. 6–7%, the math slightly favors investing — especially in a TFSA (tax-free return vs. taxable mortgage saving) or RRSP (deductible contribution + tax-deferred growth).

But the expected return is uncertain. The mortgage paydown is guaranteed.

## The Tax Angle

RRSP contributions generate a tax deduction. If you're in a 40% marginal bracket and contribute $10,000 to your RRSP, you receive $4,000 back in your tax refund. Using that refund to accelerate mortgage payments effectively combines both strategies.

**The RRSP-mortgage hybrid approach:**
1. Contribute the maximum to RRSP (get the deduction)
2. Apply the tax refund directly to your mortgage principal
3. The combined effect often beats either strategy alone

For those in the 43% bracket: a $10,000 RRSP contribution yields $4,300 refund → applied to mortgage, you've effectively contributed to RRSP AND paid down $4,300 of mortgage.

## Why Carrying a Mortgage Into Retirement Is Problematic

Even if the math is marginal, there are non-mathematical reasons to be mortgage-free at retirement:

**Fixed income = fixed tolerance for fixed costs.** In retirement, your income is more predictable but less flexible. A $2,000/month mortgage payment is a significant slice of $5,000/month in income. Without it, your budget flexibility is much higher.

**Sequence of returns risk.** In a down market, you may want to reduce portfolio withdrawals. That's harder if you have a mandatory mortgage payment every month.

**Peace of mind.** Research consistently shows retirees report higher satisfaction and security when mortgage-free. There's real value to not having your home at risk.

**Income requirements.** A mortgage payment requires more retirement income, which requires more RRSP/RRIF withdrawals, which means more taxes.

## The Middle Path: Accelerate, Don't Obsess

For most Canadians, the right answer is neither "ignore the mortgage" nor "throw everything at it." It's:

1. Max TFSA every year (always)
2. Contribute to RRSP to at least capture the deduction at your marginal rate
3. Use RRSP refunds and any surplus cash to make lump-sum mortgage payments
4. Aim to be mortgage-free by retirement — but don't sacrifice tax-sheltered room to get there

This approach optimizes both the tax efficiency of registered savings and the peace-of-mind benefit of debt reduction.

## What If You're 5 Years From Retirement With Mortgage Remaining?

The 5-year window before retirement is when the math changes most clearly in favor of mortgage paydown:

- You have less time for investment compounding to work
- Sequence of returns risk is highest in the years just before and after retirement
- The certainty of eliminating a fixed cost is more valuable than uncertain additional investment returns
- Most people find they sleep better with a clear path to being debt-free

A common 5-year plan: switch to accelerated biweekly payments, add annual lump-sum payments of 10–15% of principal (as most Canadian mortgages allow), and be mortgage-free on the day you retire.
