# Should You Buy an Annuity in Canada? A Plain-English Guide

An annuity is a contract where you hand a lump sum to an insurance company and they pay you a guaranteed monthly income — either for a fixed term or for the rest of your life. At first glance, it sounds perfect for retirement. In practice, it's a tool with significant trade-offs.

## How Annuities Work in Canada

You purchase an annuity from a life insurance company (Sun Life, Manulife, Canada Life, Desjardins, etc.) using either registered funds (RRSP, RRIF) or non-registered savings.

The insurer calculates your monthly payment based on:
- The amount you invest (the "premium")
- Current interest rates (higher rates = better annuity payments)
- Your age and gender
- The annuity type and features you choose

Payments are guaranteed — the insurer is legally required to pay you regardless of market performance.

## Types of Annuities

**Life annuity:** Pays until you die. If you live to 100, you keep collecting. If you die at 67, payments stop. This is the purest form of longevity insurance.

**Life annuity with guarantee period:** Pays for the longer of your lifetime or a guaranteed period (e.g., 10 or 15 years). If you die in year 3, your estate gets the remaining 7 years of payments. Provides peace of mind but reduces the monthly amount.

**Joint-and-survivor annuity:** Covers two lives (typically spouses). Payments continue until the last survivor dies, often at 60–100% of the original amount. Essential if your spouse has no independent income.

**Term certain annuity:** Pays for a fixed period (e.g., 20 years) regardless of whether you're alive. Not longevity insurance — used to fill a specific income gap.

**Prescribed annuity:** Purchased with non-registered funds. Tax rules spread the taxable portion evenly over the annuity's life, which can be more tax-efficient than RRIF withdrawals.

## The Core Trade-Off

**Annuity upside:**
- Guaranteed income you can't outlive
- Eliminates market risk and sequence-of-returns risk
- Simplifies retirement — no investment decisions required
- Provides psychological security

**Annuity downside:**
- No access to the capital once you've purchased
- If you die early, the insurance company keeps the remainder
- Payments don't fully keep pace with inflation (unless you buy inflation-indexed, which pays less initially)
- Once locked in, you can't take advantage of rising interest rates
- Heirs receive nothing from the annuitized funds

## Current Annuity Rates (2026)

Annuity rates are directly tied to long-term government bond yields. With interest rates higher than the 2010s lows:

A 65-year-old man investing $250,000 in a life annuity can expect roughly **$1,350–$1,450/month** for life.

A 65-year-old woman investing $250,000 gets roughly **$1,250–$1,350/month** (lower because women have longer life expectancy).

Joint annuity (couple, both 65): $250,000 buys approximately **$1,100–$1,200/month** continuing at 60% to survivor.

These rates are meaningfully better than they were in 2021, when rates were near zero.

## Annuities vs. RRIF Drawdown

Most Canadian retirees use a RRIF (Registered Retirement Income Fund) instead of an annuity. The comparison:

| Factor | Annuity | RRIF |
|---|---|---|
| Income guarantee | Guaranteed for life | Depends on returns + longevity |
| Market exposure | None | Full exposure |
| Capital access | None | Full access |
| Death benefit | Limited (depends on type) | Balance passes to estate |
| Inflation protection | Limited | Investments can outpace inflation |
| Flexibility | None once purchased | Full flexibility |

**The RRIF beats the annuity if:** You live to an average or below-average lifespan, markets perform well, and you have a bequest motive.

**The annuity beats the RRIF if:** You live well past average lifespan, markets underperform, or you lack the discipline/desire to manage investments in retirement.

## The "Annuity Puzzle"

Economists have long puzzled over why people don't buy more annuities. The math suggests life annuities are underutilized — longevity risk is real and pooling it through insurance is rational.

The main behavioural barriers:
1. Loss aversion (feels like gambling that you'll die "early")
2. Bequest motives (wanting to leave money to children)
3. Illiquidity fear (what if I need the capital?)
4. Distrust of insurance companies

## A Middle-Ground Approach

Rather than all-or-nothing, many financial planners recommend **partial annuitization**: annuitize enough to cover essential expenses (housing, food, healthcare) alongside CPP and OAS, then invest the rest in a RRIF for discretionary spending and estate.

**Example:** If you need $4,000/month to cover essential expenses:
- CPP: $1,100/month
- OAS: $750/month
- Gap: $2,150/month

Purchase an annuity paying $2,150/month with a portion of RRSP savings, then invest the remainder in a RRIF for discretionary use and legacy.

This approach eliminates longevity risk on essentials while preserving flexibility and bequest potential.

## Tax Treatment

**RRSP/RRIF funds:** Annuity payments are fully taxable as income, just like RRIF withdrawals. No special treatment.

**Non-registered funds:** Prescribed annuities have a more favourable tax structure — only the "interest" portion is taxable, not the capital return. For non-registered savings in high amounts, this can be significantly more tax-efficient than RRIF withdrawals.

**Pension income credit:** Annuity payments from an RRSP annuity purchased at 65+ qualify for the $2,000 federal pension income tax credit and are eligible for pension income splitting with a spouse.

## Is an Annuity Right for You?

Consider an annuity if:
- You're worried about outliving your savings
- You have limited CPP/DB pension income relative to expenses
- You dislike investment risk and decision-making
- You're in good health and have family history of longevity
- You don't have a strong bequest motive

Avoid or minimize annuities if:
- You have poor health (shorter expected lifespan)
- You have significant CPP/OAS/pension income already covering essentials
- You have dependents who would benefit from the capital
- Rates are low (wait for higher rates)

Shop multiple insurers — annuity quotes vary by 5–10% between companies. Use an independent broker who can access quotes from all major insurers.
