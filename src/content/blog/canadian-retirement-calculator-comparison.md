Searching for a Canadian retirement calculator is frustrating. Most results are American tools with no CPP/OAS support. The ones that are Canadian are often bank-sponsored tools designed to tell you to save more with them.

Here's an honest assessment of what's available.

## What Makes a Calculator "Canadian Enough"

A useful Canadian retirement calculator must handle:
- CPP and OAS with actual 2025 benefit amounts and deferral options
- Real Canadian tax brackets (federal + provincial, not just federal)
- RRSP/RRIF mandatory conversion and minimum withdrawals
- TFSA (and the fact that withdrawals don't count as income)
- Province-specific rates (Ontario, BC, Alberta are very different)

Without these, you're doing rough math that could be off by $200,000 or more.

## The Bank Calculator Problem

TD, RBC, BMO, and most major banks offer retirement calculators. They share a common flaw: they're designed to show you a "shortfall" that motivates you to buy their products. The output is almost always "you need to save more" regardless of actual circumstances.

They also typically:
- Ignore CPP and OAS entirely or use round numbers
- Use a single flat tax rate instead of graduated brackets
- Don't model RRIF mandatory minimums
- Have no province selector

They're better than nothing, but not by much.

## Government Calculators (Free)

**Service Canada CPP Estimator:** The best source for your actual CPP entitlement. Log in at My Service Canada Account and get a projection based on your actual contribution history. This is the number to use in any other tool.

**ESDC Retirement Calculator:** The government's own tool is basic but uses real CPP/OAS numbers. It doesn't model tax in detail but gives you a ballpark income picture.

**Limitations:** No tax modeling, no RRIF/RRSP optimization, no scenario comparison.

## Popular Free Tools

**Equisoft/Plan (formerly NAVIPLAN Lite):** Used by many financial advisors. A consumer-facing version exists but is limited. Handles Canadian specifics reasonably well.

**Wealthsimple Retirement Calculator:** Improved in recent years. Handles TFSA and RRSP. Tax modeling is simplified. No province selector. Good for a 5-minute estimate.

**Sun Life My Retirement Planner:** Better than most bank tools. Includes CPP/OAS. Limited on tax detail and no province-specific rates.

## Paid Tools

**MaxiFi Planner:** Technically excellent, economically rigorous, but primarily American. Canadian version is limited.

**Cascades:** A professional-grade Canadian tool. Used by financial planners. Not consumer-facing.

**PlannerPlus:** Another advisor-focused tool. Comprehensive but not available to consumers directly.

## What to Check in Any Calculator

When evaluating a retirement calculator, run it through these sanity checks:

1. **Does it ask which province?** If not, the tax numbers are wrong.
2. **Can you set a CPP start age other than 65?** If not, it's missing a key variable.
3. **Does it show you year-by-year income, not just a single "will you run out" answer?** The year-by-year view reveals problems the aggregate view hides.
4. **Does it distinguish RRSP/RRIF from TFSA withdrawals in its tax calculation?** A tool that taxes TFSA withdrawals is fundamentally broken.
