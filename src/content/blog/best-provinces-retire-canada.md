# Best Provinces to Retire in Canada: Tax & Cost Comparison

Where you retire in Canada matters enormously for your finances. Provincial income tax rates vary by nearly 10 percentage points, and that gap compounds over a 25-year retirement. Here's how the major provinces stack up.

## Provincial Tax Rates on Retirement Income

Provincial income tax is layered on top of federal tax. Here are the top marginal rates (2025) and what middle-income retirees actually pay:

| Province | Top Marginal Rate | Tax on $60,000 Income |
|---|---|---|
| Alberta | 48% | ~$11,800 |
| British Columbia | 53.5% | ~$13,200 |
| Ontario | 53.53% | ~$13,400 |
| Quebec | 53.31% | ~$14,900 |
| New Brunswick | 52.5% | ~$13,800 |
| Nova Scotia | 54% | ~$14,100 |

*Note: Most retirees draw $40,000–$80,000/year. Top marginal rates apply above $250,000 federally.*

Alberta consistently wins on provincial income tax — there's no provincial sales tax (GST only) and the flat provincial rate is the lowest in Canada.

## Alberta: The Tax-Friendly Choice

Alberta charges a flat 10% provincial income tax on the first $148,269 of income (2025), then 12%, 13%, 14%, or 15% on higher brackets. For most retirees, you'll pay around 10–12% provincial tax.

Add no provincial sales tax (PST) and you keep significantly more of every dollar.

**The catch:** Alberta's healthcare premiums were eliminated in 2009, but the province has faced boom-bust cycles affecting public services. Property taxes in Calgary and Edmonton are moderate.

## British Columbia: Climate Premium

BC offers mild weather (especially Victoria and the Okanagan) but at a price. Housing costs are among Canada's highest, though interior communities like Kamloops and Kelowna are more affordable.

Provincial income tax starts at 5.06% and climbs to 20.5% on income over $252,752. For a retiree drawing $60,000/year, BC's tax burden is moderate — better than Quebec, worse than Alberta.

BC's **BC Senior's Supplement** adds up to $49.30/month for low-income seniors on GIS.

## Ontario: The Middle Ground

Ontario is where most Canadians already live, so the question is whether to stay or move. With a 9.15% basic provincial rate and 11.16% above $102,135, Ontario's taxes are middle of the pack.

The **Ontario Guaranteed Annual Income System (GAINS)** tops up low-income seniors by up to $166/month. Property taxes vary hugely — Toronto levies around 0.66% annually while smaller cities can exceed 1.5%.

Ontario has universal drug coverage for seniors 65+ through the ODB program, which can save thousands annually.

## Quebec: High Tax, High Service

Quebec has the highest provincial income taxes but also the most comprehensive public services. Dental care, subsidized childcare (relevant if you have grandchildren nearby), and robust social programs partially offset the tax cost.

If you have a defined benefit pension or significant RRSP/RRIF withdrawals, Quebec's top provincial rate of 25.75% on income over $119,910 adds up. A retiree drawing $80,000/year pays roughly $4,000–$5,000 more in provincial tax than an Albertan counterpart.

However, Quebec's **subsidized home care** programs and CLSC network reduce out-of-pocket healthcare costs that other provinces leave to individuals.

## Maritime Provinces: Affordable But Cold

New Brunswick, Nova Scotia, and PEI offer some of Canada's lowest real estate prices. A comfortable 3-bedroom home in Moncton or Fredericton can be had for $300,000–$400,000, compared to $800,000+ in Vancouver or Toronto.

The trade-off: limited employment options (irrelevant in retirement), harsher winters, and fewer specialist healthcare providers. The Maritimes are also experiencing population growth as retirees discover the value — prices are rising.

Nova Scotia levies a combined federal-provincial top rate of 54%, the highest in Canada, but most retirees won't approach top-bracket income.

## What Really Drives the Decision

**Tax savings vs. housing costs.** Alberta's tax advantage over Ontario is roughly $1,500–$2,500/year for a typical retiree. But moving from Toronto to Calgary doesn't automatically save money if you're selling a house worth $1.2M and buying one worth $700,000 — that $500,000 in freed equity earns income regardless.

**Healthcare access.** All provinces provide universal healthcare, but specialist wait times and proximity to major hospitals matter as you age. Large urban centres in Ontario and BC have more hospital capacity per capita than smaller Maritime cities.

**Family proximity.** The most financially optimal province often loses to where your children and grandchildren live. Don't underestimate the value of proximity.

**Climate preferences.** BC's lower mainland and Vancouver Island get 50% more sunny days than central Canada in winter. For many retirees, this alone justifies a higher cost of living.

## Running the Numbers for Your Situation

A full comparison should model:

1. Provincial income tax on your expected retirement income
2. Provincial sales tax (Saskatchewan, Manitoba, Ontario, Quebec, Maritime provinces all charge HST/PST)
3. Property tax on your expected home value
4. Healthcare supplement programs you'd qualify for
5. Capital gains on any home sale and provincial tax treatment

## Bottom Line

For pure tax efficiency: **Alberta** wins, especially for retirees with $60,000–$120,000/year in income.

For balance of lifestyle, healthcare access, and reasonable taxes: **BC interior or Ontario** remain competitive.

For affordable housing with relaxed coastal living: **Maritime provinces**, particularly PEI and New Brunswick, are increasingly compelling.

The "best" province is the one that maximizes your total retirement quality — not just your after-tax income.
