Canada allows eligible couples to split up to 50% of qualifying pension income on their tax returns. This is one of the most underutilized tax strategies available to Canadian retirees.

## What Qualifies as Eligible Pension Income?

**At age 65+:**
- RRIF withdrawals (including LIF and PRIF)
- Annuity payments from an RRSP or DPSP
- Defined benefit pension payments
- Registered pension plan payments

**At any age:**
- Defined benefit pension payments (if under 65, only DB pensions qualify)

**Does not qualify:**
- CPP payments
- OAS payments
- Non-registered investment income

## How It Works

You and your spouse elect to split on your joint tax return (T1032 form). No money actually moves between bank accounts — it's purely a tax election. You allocate up to 50% of your eligible pension income to your spouse, who reports it on their return.

**Example:**
- Partner A: $85,000 income ($60,000 RRIF + $25,000 CPP/OAS) — Ontario marginal rate: 43.41%
- Partner B: $25,000 income (OAS + part-time work) — Ontario marginal rate: 20.05%
- Split: $30,000 of RRIF income from A to B
- A's new income: $55,000 — rate drops to 29.65%
- B's new income: $55,000 — rate increases to 29.65% (still a combined win)

**Combined tax saving:** The differential between 43.41% and ~29.65% on $30,000 = ~$4,128/year.

## The Age Amount Optimization

At 65, the federal age amount is $8,396 (2025), phasing out above ~$42,335. If the lower-income spouse's income is below this threshold, shifting pension income to them may accidentally reduce their age amount.

The optimal split often involves testing multiple allocations to find the income level that preserves the age amount while still benefiting from the lower bracket.

## Provincial Considerations

Pension splitting works at the federal level. Ontario (and most provinces) also recognize the split for provincial tax purposes, making the combined saving larger in high-rate provinces.

In Ontario, income above $100,000 faces a provincial surtax of 20% on Ontario tax above $5,315 (2025), and 56% on Ontario tax above $6,802. Splitting income to stay below these surtax thresholds can save additional hundreds of dollars.

## CPP Sharing vs. Pension Splitting

These are two separate mechanisms often confused:

**CPP sharing:** Both spouses apply to share their CPP payments. Actual CPP payments are redistributed to equalize income. A formal application to Service Canada is required.

**Pension income splitting:** A tax return election only. No Service Canada application, no change to actual payments.

You can use both simultaneously, but the interactions are worth modeling — CPP sharing reduces the higher-earning spouse's actual CPP income, which reduces the base for pension splitting.

## When Splitting Doesn't Help

- When both spouses have similar incomes already
- When the lower-income spouse would lose income-tested benefits (GIS, provincial benefits) by receiving more income
- When the age amount phase-out creates a notch effect that offsets some of the bracket benefit

## Steps to Claim

1. Complete Form T1032 — Joint Election to Split Pension Income
2. Both spouses sign the form
3. Report the split on both returns — the transferring spouse claims a deduction (line 21000), the receiving spouse reports the income (line 11600)

The form is included in most Canadian tax software (TurboTax, Wealthsimple Tax, etc.) and it takes about 5 minutes.
