If you work in the public sector, healthcare, or a large unionized employer, you likely have a choice — or have already made the choice — between a defined benefit (DB) and defined contribution (DC) pension. The answer isn't obvious, and the stakes are high.

## What Is a Defined Benefit Pension?

A DB pension promises a specific monthly income in retirement, typically based on years of service and a final or average salary formula.

Common formula: **2% × years of service × final average salary**

Example: 30 years × 2% × $85,000 = **$51,000/year** at retirement.

That income is guaranteed for life, often inflation-indexed, and typically includes a survivor benefit for your spouse.

The employer (and the pension fund manager) bears the investment risk. If the fund underperforms, the employer (or government) must top it up.

## What Is a Defined Contribution Pension?

A DC pension is essentially a registered savings plan. You and your employer contribute a set percentage (e.g., 5% employer + 5% employee = 10% of salary). The money is invested — typically in mutual funds you choose.

At retirement, the balance in your account is what you have. It can be converted to a Life Income Fund (LIF) or used to purchase an annuity.

The employee bears the investment risk.

## The Value of a DB Pension

DB pensions are often undervalued because employees don't think about what it would cost to replicate the income in the market.

**To replicate $51,000/year of inflation-indexed lifetime income privately:**

At current annuity rates, a 65-year-old male would need approximately **$900,000–$1,100,000** to purchase a comparable indexed annuity. A couple with survivor benefits might need $1,300,000+.

Most employees contributing to a DB plan for 30 years contribute far less than this in total contributions (employee + employer). The difference is subsidized by taxpayers (public sector) or represents the employer's competitive compensation advantage.

**Rule of thumb:** A DB pension paying $1,000/month is worth approximately $200,000–$250,000 in capital value at retirement.

## The Tradeoffs

### DB Advantages
- Guaranteed income for life — no longevity risk
- Employer bears investment risk
- Often inflation-indexed
- Survivor benefits protect your spouse
- No investment management required
- Predictable retirement planning

### DB Disadvantages
- Vesting periods can be long (5–10 years before you're entitled)
- Not portable — changing employers often means reduced pension
- If the employer or fund fails, your pension may be at risk (less so in public sector, more so in private sector)
- No estate value — income stops on death (beyond survivor benefits)
- Inflexible — you receive the formula amount, not more

### DC Advantages
- Portable — takes your balance with you when you change jobs
- Full estate value — your balance passes to heirs
- Control over investments
- Potentially higher value if markets perform strongly

### DC Disadvantages
- You bear investment risk
- Longevity risk — you can outlive the balance
- Requires investment knowledge and discipline
- Volatile — market downturns near retirement can significantly reduce your retirement income

## For Canadians With DB Pensions: The Planning Implications

If you have a substantial DB pension, your retirement planning is different:

**RRSP strategy shifts:** Your pension may push your retirement income above OAS clawback thresholds. Prioritizing TFSA over RRSP in later career years makes more sense.

**CPP deferral makes more sense:** With DB income covering your base, you can afford to defer CPP to 70 for the 42% bonus.

**Pension income splitting is available:** DB pension payments are eligible pension income for splitting purposes at any age (post-retirement). This is a significant advantage for high-pension couples.

**The commuted value option:** Some pensions offer a lump-sum commuted value option at departure. Unless you're leaving for a portable DC plan and the commuted value is very high, taking the monthly pension is usually better for most people.
