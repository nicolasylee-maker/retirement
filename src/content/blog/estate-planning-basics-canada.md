Estate planning is not just for the wealthy. It's for anyone who has assets, dependents, or preferences about what happens to their money when they die.

In Canada, there's no estate tax — but there is a deemed disposition on death that can result in significant income tax. Here's what you need to know.

## The Core Documents

### Will

A will directs who receives your assets, names an executor, and designates guardians for minor children. Without a will ("intestate"), provincial intestacy laws determine distribution — which may not match your wishes.

In Ontario, dying without a will means your estate is distributed according to the *Succession Law Reform Act* — spouse gets the first $350,000 and shares the rest with children. Your adult children may receive equal portions of assets you wanted to leave to specific heirs.

A basic will drafted by a lawyer: $300–$800. A complex will with trusts for minor children or business interests: $1,500–$5,000.

### Power of Attorney for Property (POA)

Authorizes someone to manage your financial affairs if you become incapacitated. Without this, family members must apply to court for guardianship — a costly and slow process.

### Power of Attorney for Personal Care (Healthcare Directive)

Authorizes someone to make healthcare decisions on your behalf. Specifies your wishes around life-sustaining treatment, organ donation, etc.

All three documents are typically done together. If you don't have them, this is the most important action item from this article.

## How Assets Transfer on Death

**RRSP/RRIF with named beneficiary:** Transfers directly to the named beneficiary, bypassing the estate and probate. If the beneficiary is a spouse, it rolls over tax-free to their RRSP/RRIF. If the beneficiary is anyone else (child, sibling, estate), the full value is included in the deceased's income and taxed in the final return.

**TFSA with named beneficiary (Successor Holder or Beneficiary):** If the spouse is named as Successor Holder, the TFSA transfers to them intact — tax-free, no income inclusion, no loss of their own TFSA room. If anyone else is named, the TFSA is paid to them tax-free, but future growth after death to date of distribution may be taxable.

**Real estate:** Deemed to be disposed of at fair market value on death. Capital gains on the principal residence are exempt. Capital gains on rental or vacation properties are fully taxable.

**Non-registered investments:** Deemed disposed at FMV. Capital gains are taxable in the final return.

**Bank accounts and non-registered investments with a named beneficiary or joint owner:** Pass outside the estate, avoiding probate.

## Probate in Ontario

Probate (Certificate of Appointment of Estate Trustee) validates your will and gives the executor legal authority to act. In Ontario, the fee is 1.5% of the estate value above $50,000.

On a $1,000,000 estate: probate fee = ~$14,250.

Assets that pass outside the estate (RRSP/RRIF with named beneficiary, TFSA, joint accounts) avoid probate. Holding more assets in TFSA and RRSP with beneficiary designations reduces the probate fee.

## The RRIF Tax on Death

One of the most significant estate planning issues for Canadians: RRIF balances.

If your RRIF is $400,000 at death and your spouse has predeceased you, the entire $400,000 is added to your income in the final tax return. In Ontario at high income, the marginal rate exceeds 50%. Tax bill: $200,000+.

Strategies to reduce this:
- Draw down RRIF more aggressively in later life (above minimums)
- Name a spouse as beneficiary for tax-deferred rollover
- In some cases, annuitize a portion of RRIF to reduce estate exposure

## Beneficiary Designations: Review Annually

Many Canadians set beneficiary designations once and forget them. Common problems:
- Ex-spouse is still named after divorce
- Minor children are named (creating trust administration complications)
- Estate is named instead of individuals (triggering probate on RRSP/RRIF)
- No beneficiary named at all

Review designations after: marriage, divorce, birth of children or grandchildren, or death of a named beneficiary.
