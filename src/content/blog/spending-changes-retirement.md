Retirement calculators ask for your expected monthly spending. Most people guess. That guess — high or low by $1,000–$2,000/month — changes the required savings by $300,000–$600,000.

Getting this right matters more than almost any other variable.

## Why Spending Is the Most Important Input

Consider two scenarios at 65, with identical CPP + OAS of $2,500/month:

**Scenario A:** Spending $4,500/month. Gap: $2,000/month. Annual gap: $24,000. Savings needed at 4% withdrawal: $600,000.

**Scenario B:** Spending $6,500/month. Gap: $4,000/month. Annual gap: $48,000. Savings needed at 4% withdrawal: $1,200,000.

Same income. Same everything — except spending. The difference in required savings: **$600,000**.

That's why the spending estimate is the most consequential input in any retirement plan.

## How Spending Actually Changes in Retirement

The academic research on retirement spending shows a consistent pattern: the "retirement spending smile."

**Early retirement (ages 60–75): The Go-Go Years**
Spending is often *higher* than pre-retirement. Travel, hobbies, home renovations, helping adult children. Many new retirees spend more than they did while working.

**Mid-retirement (ages 75–85): The Slow-Go Years**
Spending drops. Travel slows. Big-ticket purchases decline. This is when many retirees find they're spending 15–25% less than in their early retirement years.

**Late retirement (ages 85+): The No-Go Years**
Spending drops significantly on most discretionary categories, but healthcare costs can spike sharply — especially for Ontarians needing assisted living or long-term care.

The mistake: planning for constant inflation-adjusted spending from 65 to 95. Real spending is highest early, dips in the middle, then spikes unpredictably at the end.

## What to Include in Your Retirement Budget

**Fixed costs:**
- Housing (rent/mortgage, property tax, utilities, maintenance)
- Insurance (home, auto, supplemental health)
- Food and groceries
- Transportation
- Medications

**Variable but predictable:**
- Travel (set a realistic annual budget)
- Dining and entertainment
- Clothing
- Gifts and charitable giving

**Irregular but certain:**
- Home repairs and maintenance (budget 1–2% of home value annually)
- Vehicle replacement every 8–12 years
- Dental and vision (often not covered after losing group benefits)

**Unpredictable:**
- Long-term care (average Ontario LTC cost: $3,000–$7,000/month depending on type)
- Supporting family members
- Major health events

## The Ontario Long-Term Care Reality

Long-term care is the biggest wildcard in Ontario retirement planning. Current government-subsidized LTC costs $2,145–$2,701/month for a basic room. Private facilities run $4,000–$8,000/month.

A 2-year LTC stay costs $48,000–$192,000. Average Ontario LTC stay is about 2.5 years.

This doesn't mean you need to save for the worst case — it means the worst case should factor into your retirement plan somewhere, whether as an insurance product or a specific reserve in your TFSA.

## How to Actually Estimate Your Spending

The most reliable method: track actual spending for 3–6 months before retirement. Add the costs you'll gain (travel, hobbies) and subtract costs you'll lose (commuting, work clothes, saving contributions).

Then build three scenarios:
- **Base case:** What you realistically expect to spend
- **Conservative:** Base + 20% for the unexpected
- **Lean:** What you could cut to if markets underperform

Running all three through a retirement calculator shows you the range of outcomes and which assumptions matter most.

## The Spending Lever Is Powerful Both Ways

The spending number isn't fixed. A retiree who discovers they've over-saved can increase spending — travel more, give more, help family. A retiree who finds spending too high has real options: downsize the home, move to a lower-cost city, cut discretionary spending.

The goal of retirement planning isn't to maximize the size of your estate. It's to match your spending to your resources in a way that lets you live the retirement you actually want.
