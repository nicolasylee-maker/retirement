Most Canadians assume their spouse will receive a significant CPP survivor benefit when they die. The reality is often disappointing — and the surprise can derail retirement plans built on that assumption.

Here's how the CPP survivor pension actually works.

## The Basic Survivor Benefit

When a CPP contributor dies, their surviving spouse may receive the **CPP survivor's pension**. The amount depends on:

1. How much CPP the deceased was receiving (or entitled to receive)
2. The survivor's own age
3. Whether the survivor is already receiving their own CPP

**The formula for survivors age 65+:**

The survivor receives **60% of the deceased contributor's CPP retirement pension** — but only to a maximum.

The combined maximum of your own CPP + survivor CPP cannot exceed the maximum CPP retirement pension ($1,364/month in 2025).

## The Combination Limit Problem

This is where most people are surprised.

If both spouses have high CPP entitlements, the combination limit means the survivor receives little or no survivor pension on top of their own.

**Example:**
- Husband's CPP: $1,200/month
- Wife's CPP: $900/month
- Maximum allowed combined: $1,364/month
- Survivor benefit formula: 60% × $1,200 = $720/month
- Wife's combined would be: $900 + $720 = $1,620/month
- But maximum is $1,364/month
- So wife actually receives: $1,364/month total

She gets only $464/month more than her own CPP — not the $720/month survivor benefit. She loses $256/month due to the combination cap.

## Survivors Under 65

For surviving spouses under 65, the formula is different:

**A flat rate plus 37.5% of the contributor's CPP retirement pension.**

In 2025, the flat rate is $228.22/month. So a 60-year-old survivor whose spouse received $1,000/month CPP would receive: $228.22 + (37.5% × $1,000) = $228.22 + $375 = **$603.22/month**.

This is still subject to the combination limit if the survivor is also receiving CPP.

## Children's Benefits

If the deceased contributor had dependent children (under 18, or 18–25 in full-time school), each eligible child receives $294.12/month (2025). This is separate from the survivor pension and not subject to the combination limit.

## Death Benefit

A one-time lump sum of **$2,500** (the maximum, and most contributors receive exactly this amount). This is not income-tested and is paid to the estate. It barely covers a funeral.

## Planning Implications

**For couples with both high CPP entitlements:** Don't plan as if the survivor will receive 60% of your CPP on top of their own. Model the combination limit realistically.

**The OAS offset:** OAS is not affected by death. If both spouses receive OAS, the survivor loses the deceased spouse's OAS entirely. The surviving spouse receives only their own OAS. This $8,724/year loss (2025 maximum OAS) is often overlooked.

**CPP + OAS income drop on first death:** For a couple with both maxed CPP and OAS, the surviving spouse's income can drop by $20,000–$24,000/year. Plan for this transition.

**Life insurance fills the gap:** For many couples, term or permanent life insurance is a more reliable way to ensure the survivor maintains their income than relying on CPP survivor benefits.
