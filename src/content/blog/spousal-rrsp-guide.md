A spousal RRSP is one of the few remaining income-splitting strategies available to Canadian couples. Used correctly, it can save thousands in combined taxes each year in retirement.

## What Is a Spousal RRSP?

A spousal RRSP is an RRSP account registered in your spouse's name but funded by your contributions. You get the tax deduction (at your marginal rate), but your spouse owns the account and withdraws from it in retirement at their (lower) marginal rate.

The two key rules:
1. **Contributions count against the contributor's RRSP room**, not the spouse's room
2. **The 3-year attribution rule:** If the spouse withdraws within 3 calendar years of the last contribution, the withdrawal is taxed as the contributor's income, not the spouse's

## When It Makes Sense

Spousal RRSPs are most valuable when:

**Significant income disparity expected in retirement.** If you'll have a large RRSP, pension, CPP, and OAS — while your spouse will have only OAS and modest savings — spousal RRSP contributions shift assets to the lower-income spouse now.

**You want income beyond what pension splitting allows.** Pension income splitting (RRIF withdrawals) is available at 65. Spousal RRSP withdrawals can be taken before 65, allowing income splitting earlier in retirement.

**Your spouse is significantly younger.** Their RRSP compounds for longer, and RRIF conversion is delayed by their age (you can base minimum withdrawals on their age).

**You're in the top tax bracket.** The deduction at 43–53% is worth more. Withdrawals at your spouse's 20–29% bracket creates a real tax differential.

## The 3-Year Attribution Rule

The rule exists to prevent immediate abuse of the strategy. If your spouse withdraws in the same calendar year as a contribution, or in the next 2 calendar years, the withdrawal is attributed back to you.

**Critical point:** The rule is based on calendar years, not 12-month periods.

If you contribute in December 2025, the 3-year calendar window is 2025, 2026, 2027. Your spouse can withdraw tax-free (attributed to them) from January 1, 2028 — even though it's only 25 months later.

If you plan to retire and have your spouse draw from the spousal RRSP, make the last contribution at least 3 calendar years before the planned withdrawal year.

## Spousal RRSP vs Pension Income Splitting

These strategies overlap but aren't identical:

**Pension income splitting:** Available at 65+, covers RRIF withdrawals, split up to 50% — but the income still originates from the higher earner.

**Spousal RRSP withdrawals:** Can occur before 65. Withdrawals are genuinely the spouse's income (after the attribution period). Useful for pre-65 income splitting.

For retirees before 65, spousal RRSP is the primary income-splitting mechanism available.

## How Much to Contribute

The goal: equalize RRSP/RRIF income in retirement between spouses. The target is having each spouse draw roughly the same income from registered accounts, keeping both in the same (lower) tax bracket.

If you project your RRIF minimum at 72 will be $60,000 and your spouse will have $15,000, direct spousal RRSP contributions until their projected RRIF generates $30,000–$40,000 to equalize.

This projection requires modeling, but rough income parity between spouses at retirement is the goal.

## Opening a Spousal RRSP

Open a separate RRSP account in your spouse's name, designated as "Spousal RRSP." Available at all major banks, credit unions, and discount brokerages. The investment options are identical to a personal RRSP.

Your annual tax slip (T4RSP or T4A) will reflect the contribution. Use Schedule 7 to claim the RRSP deduction.

The spousal RRSP converts to a spousal RRIF at 71 (your spouse's age), following the same rules as a personal RRIF.
