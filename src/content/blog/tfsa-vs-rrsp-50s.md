You're in your 50s. You have limited savings capacity. TFSA and RRSP both have room. Which do you prioritize?

The answer depends on one key comparison: your marginal tax rate today vs. your expected marginal tax rate in retirement.

## The Core Logic

**RRSP is better when:** You're in a higher tax bracket now than you'll be in retirement. You get a big deduction today and pay less tax on withdrawal later.

**TFSA is better when:** Your retirement income will be similar to or higher than your current income — or when the tax-free nature of withdrawals protects specific income-tested benefits like OAS.

In your 50s, this comparison has a specific Canadian context.

## The OAS Clawback Factor

This is the biggest reason many Canadians in their 50s should prioritize TFSA over RRSP: the OAS clawback.

If your RRSP grows large enough that RRIF minimum withdrawals at 72+ push your income above $90,997 (2025 threshold), you'll lose 15 cents of OAS per dollar over that threshold. TFSA withdrawals don't count as income and never trigger the clawback.

A large RRSP can actually reduce your total retirement income after the clawback — making the RRSP contribution less valuable than it appeared at age 55.

## When RRSP Wins in Your 50s

**You're in the top brackets.** If your marginal rate is 43%+ (Ontario income above ~$100,000), the RRSP deduction is worth a lot today. The tax deferral is most valuable at high marginal rates.

**You expect significantly lower retirement income.** If you'll have a modest pension or CPP, your RRIF withdrawals won't push you into high brackets. The RRSP deduction today vs. lower-bracket withdrawal later is a genuine win.

**You have a pension.** If your defined benefit pension will cover your living expenses, your RRSP can act as a buffer for irregular expenses or legacy goals — and you can time withdrawals to stay in lower brackets.

## When TFSA Wins in Your 50s

**You've already contributed significantly to your RRSP.** A large existing RRSP means future mandatory RRIF withdrawals will be substantial. Adding more to RRSP now makes the future tax problem larger.

**Your income in retirement will be similar to today's.** If you'll have CPP, OAS, a pension, and RRIF income — your marginal rate in retirement may not be much lower than today. The RRSP deduction advantage shrinks or disappears.

**You're near the OAS clawback threshold.** If your projected retirement income is near $91,000, prioritizing TFSA now keeps future income lower and protects your full OAS.

**Your spouse has lower income.** If your spouse earns significantly less, consider spousal RRSP contributions instead of your own RRSP — and prioritize TFSA for yourself.

## The Practical Answer for Most 50-Year-Olds

For most Canadians in their 50s with middle incomes ($70,000–$120,000) and existing RRSP balances:

1. **Max TFSA first** — $7,000/year in 2025, plus any unused room
2. **RRSP for the remainder** — especially if your marginal rate is above 33%

For high earners ($150,000+) with pensions:
1. **RRSP first** — the deduction is worth 43–53% today
2. **TFSA second** — to build a clawback-safe withdrawal pool

## Catch-Up TFSA Contributions

If you've never maximized your TFSA, 2025 offers cumulative room of $95,000 (for someone eligible since 2009). That's a significant opportunity in your 50s — one lump-sum transfer of investments from a non-registered account can shield decades of future growth from tax.

The catch: transferring investments in-kind to a TFSA can trigger capital gains on existing gains. Factor this in before transferring appreciated securities.

## The Bottom Line

In your 50s, prioritize TFSA if:
- You already have a substantial RRSP
- Your retirement income will be near or above the OAS clawback threshold
- Your marginal rate in retirement will be similar to today's

Prioritize RRSP if:
- You're in the top 1–2 brackets today
- You expect meaningfully lower retirement income
- You have a high-earning spouse and want to split income via spousal RRSP
