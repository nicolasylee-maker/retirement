The deadline is firm: by December 31 of the year you turn 71, your RRSP must be converted or collapsed. There are three options — and one of them results in the entire balance being added to your income in a single year.

## The Three Options

### Option 1: Convert to a RRIF (Recommended for Most)

A Registered Retirement Income Fund keeps your investments tax-sheltered and allows continued growth. You're required to withdraw a minimum each year (based on age and balance), but you can withdraw more at any time.

Benefits:
- Investments stay sheltered until withdrawn
- Minimum withdrawals can be deferred to your spouse's age if younger
- Eligible for the pension income credit at 65+
- Eligible for pension income splitting at 65+
- Estate rollover to surviving spouse is tax-free

Drawbacks:
- Mandatory minimum withdrawals increase with age
- Inflexible — once a RRIF, always a RRIF until depleted or annuity conversion

**For most Canadians, this is the default choice.**

### Option 2: Purchase an Annuity

You use some or all of your RRSP proceeds to buy a registered annuity from an insurance company. The insurer guarantees a fixed income payment for life or a fixed term.

Benefits:
- Eliminates longevity risk (you can't outlive the income)
- No investment management required
- Predictable income for budgeting

Drawbacks:
- Locks in current interest rates permanently
- No growth potential if rates rise
- No flexibility to take larger withdrawals if needed
- No estate value after death (unless guaranteed term purchased)

Annuities make the most sense at older ages (75+) when the longevity risk is highest and when interest rates are relatively high.

### Option 3: Cash Out (Almost Never Recommended)

You can withdraw the entire RRSP balance as cash. The full amount is added to your income in that calendar year.

For a $300,000 RRSP cashout in Ontario, you'd add $300,000 to income. Combined with other income, you're looking at 53.53% marginal tax on the top portion. Tax bill: potentially $100,000–$150,000 in a single year.

There's almost no scenario where cashing out entirely makes sense.

## The Deadline and Mechanics

The deadline is December 31 of the year you turn 71. If you miss it, the CRA considers your RRSP to have been cashed out on that date — the entire balance becomes income.

**What to do:**
1. Contact your financial institution in October/November of your 71st year
2. Complete the RRIF conversion paperwork
3. Your existing investments typically transfer in-kind — no selling required
4. Name a beneficiary (spouse should be primary beneficiary for the tax-deferred rollover)

First withdrawals are required the year after conversion (the year you turn 72).

## You Can Have Multiple RRSPs and RRIFs

If you have RRSPs at multiple institutions, you can convert each to a RRIF separately or consolidate. There's no limit on the number of RRIFs you can hold.

Some people maintain multiple RRIFs to keep different investment strategies (conservative bonds at one, equity growth at another) while meeting minimums from each proportionally.

## Converting Before 71: Is It Ever Smart?

Yes. Converting your RRSP to a RRIF before 71 can be strategic if:

- You want to access the pension income credit at 65 (minimum $2,000 RRIF withdrawal qualifies)
- You're doing an early RRIF draw strategy to melt down a large RRSP
- You need access to funds and want the pension income credit to reduce tax

The pension income credit alone is worth up to $300/year ($2,000 × 15%). For a couple who haven't otherwise started pension income, converting one RRSP to a RRIF at 65 and withdrawing the minimum provides the credit on both returns.
