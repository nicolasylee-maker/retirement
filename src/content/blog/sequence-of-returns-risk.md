Imagine two retirees. Both earn an average of 6% per year over 30 years. Both start with $1,000,000 and withdraw $50,000/year. One runs out of money at 85. The other still has $800,000 at 95.

The difference? The *order* of returns. Not the average.

## What Is Sequence of Returns Risk?

When you're accumulating savings, the order of returns doesn't matter much. A 20% loss followed by a 20% gain is the same as a 20% gain followed by a 20% loss — you end up at roughly the same place.

In retirement, drawing down your portfolio, the order matters enormously. Bad returns early in retirement — while your balance is at its highest and you're withdrawing — inflict permanent damage. Good returns early allow compounding to work in your favor.

This is sequence of returns risk.

## The Math That Makes It Real

**Retiree A:** Good sequence — returns of +15%, +12%, +10%... then a bad decade late in retirement.
**Retiree B:** Bad sequence — returns of -20%, -15%, -5%... then a strong recovery later.

Both average 6%. Both withdraw $50,000/year from $1,000,000.

Retiree A still has $600,000+ at 90. Retiree B runs out at 82.

Why? Because early losses force you to sell more shares at low prices to fund withdrawals. Those shares aren't available to participate in the subsequent recovery. The portfolio never fully rebounds.

## Why This Is Especially Relevant for Canadians Retiring Now

The years immediately following record-high equity markets carry elevated sequence risk. If you retire when valuations are high and a significant market correction follows in years 1–5, you face the worst possible sequence.

This doesn't mean you should delay retirement if markets are high — timing the market is a losing strategy. It means you should structure your portfolio to *manage* the risk.

## Strategies to Reduce Sequence Risk

### 1. The Two-Bucket Approach

Keep 2–3 years of living expenses in cash or short-term bonds (Bucket 1). Keep the rest in growth assets (Bucket 2).

In a market downturn, draw from Bucket 1 instead of selling equities. This gives your equity portfolio time to recover without forced selling at lows.

As markets recover, replenish Bucket 1 from Bucket 2.

### 2. Flexible Withdrawal Rates

Rather than taking a fixed $50,000/year regardless of market conditions, reduce withdrawals by 10–20% in bad market years and increase them in good years.

This sounds simple but requires discipline. The retirees who failed in the worst historical sequence scenarios were often those who couldn't or wouldn't reduce spending.

### 3. Guaranteed Income Base

CPP, OAS, and defined benefit pensions don't have sequence of returns risk — they pay regardless of what the market does. The larger your guaranteed income base relative to spending, the less you depend on portfolio returns in any single year.

This is one reason delaying CPP to 70 is an effective sequence-of-returns hedge: you increase your guaranteed income floor, reducing the portfolio withdrawal needed in any year.

### 4. Bond Tent Strategy

A "rising equity glidepath" approach: hold a higher allocation to bonds/GICs in the 5 years before and after retirement (the danger zone), then gradually shift back toward equities as you age.

This is counterintuitive — conventional wisdom says reduce equities as you age. But the sequence risk is highest right at retirement, not in late retirement.

### 5. RRSP/TFSA Sequencing

Draw from registered accounts in a tax-efficient order. In bad market years, drawing from TFSA (no tax impact) rather than selling RRSPs/RRIF at temporarily depressed prices and paying tax on the withdrawal preserves more assets for recovery.

## The Canadian Context

Canadian retirees have one significant sequence-risk buffer American retirees lack: government benefits that are guaranteed and inflation-indexed.

A retiree with $50,000/year in CPP + OAS (couple) needs their portfolio to produce much less than a retiree relying on pure portfolio income. Sequence risk on $20,000/year of portfolio withdrawals is far more manageable than sequence risk on $70,000/year.

This is another argument for maximizing your guaranteed income floor — through CPP deferral, OAS deferral, and DB pension — before relying on portfolio withdrawals.

## The Bottom Line

Sequence of returns risk is the reason the 4% safe withdrawal rate exists — it's calibrated to survive the worst historical sequences, not just average returns. If your plan only works with average returns in the right order, it's not a robust plan.

Build in buffers: a cash reserve, flexible spending, maximized guaranteed income, and a withdrawal strategy that doesn't force selling in downturns.
