Canada's retirement income system is often described as a three-legged stool: government programs, employer pensions, and personal savings. The government programs leg has three separate components that work very differently from each other.

## Tier 1: Canada Pension Plan (CPP)

**What it is:** A contributory pension plan. You earn CPP by contributing from employment income.

**Contributions:** 5.95% of insurable earnings between the basic exemption ($3,500) and the YMPE ($71,300 in 2025). Employers match your contribution. Self-employed pay both sides.

**Eligibility:** Must have made at least one valid CPP contribution. No residency requirement beyond contributing years.

**Benefit:** Based on your contribution history — the average of your best earning years. Maximum in 2025: $1,364.60/month at 65. Average paid: ~$780/month.

**Start age:** 60–70. Early = lower, delayed = higher. Indexed to CPI every January.

**The key insight:** CPP is an earned benefit, not a welfare program. The more you contributed and the longer you worked, the higher your benefit.

## Tier 2: Old Age Security (OAS)

**What it is:** A universal pension funded from general tax revenue. Not contribution-based.

**Eligibility:** Age 65+, Canadian citizen or legal resident, minimum 10 years of Canadian residence after age 18. Full pension requires 40 years of Canadian residence after 18.

**Benefit:** Full OAS in 2025: $727.67/month. Prorated if fewer than 40 years of residence.

**The OAS clawback:** At net income above $90,997 (2025), OAS is reduced by 15 cents per dollar. At ~$148,451, OAS is fully eliminated.

**Start age:** 65–70. Can be deferred for 0.6%/month (36% more at 70).

**The key insight:** OAS is a residency-based universal program. Most Canadians who have lived in Canada their adult lives qualify for full or near-full OAS regardless of their work history.

## Tier 3: Guaranteed Income Supplement (GIS)

**What it is:** A means-tested supplement for low-income OAS recipients. Not taxable.

**Eligibility:** Must be receiving OAS. Income must be below the threshold (~$22,056 for single seniors in 2025).

**Benefit:** Maximum GIS for a single senior in 2025: $1,057.01/month. Reduced by 50 cents for every dollar of other income above $0.

**With OAS + GIS combined:** A single senior with no other income receives up to $1,784.68/month in 2025 ($21,416/year). This is the government's minimum income floor for seniors who qualify.

**The GIS trap:** Because GIS is reduced by 50 cents per dollar of other income, RRSP withdrawals are extremely costly for low-income seniors. Every $1,000 in RRSP withdrawal reduces GIS by $500. The effective marginal rate including GIS clawback can exceed 70% for low-income seniors with any RRSP savings.

## How the Three Tiers Stack

For a typical Canadian retiring at 65 with average CPP:

| Source | Monthly | Annual |
|--------|---------|--------|
| CPP (average) | $780 | $9,360 |
| OAS (full) | $728 | $8,736 |
| **Total public** | **$1,508** | **$18,096** |

For a couple, both with average CPP and full OAS:
- Combined: $3,016/month ($36,192/year)

This is the guaranteed floor on top of which savings must cover the gap to your actual spending needs.

## The Immigrant and Newcomer Considerations

If you arrived in Canada as an adult, your OAS may be prorated. The formula: (years of Canadian residence after 18) ÷ 40 × full OAS.

Arriving at 30 and retiring at 65 = 35 years = 35/40 = 87.5% of full OAS.

International Social Security agreements exist between Canada and many countries, which can count foreign residency or contributions toward Canadian benefit eligibility. Check Service Canada for your specific country.

## CPP and the Self-Employed

Self-employed Canadians pay the full CPP contribution rate (11.9% in 2025, both employee and employer portions) on net self-employment income. This is expensive but builds the same CPP benefit as an employee.

The risk: many self-employed Canadians minimize their CPP contributions by structuring income as dividends (corporations) or by not tracking their net earnings accurately. This directly reduces their retirement income.
