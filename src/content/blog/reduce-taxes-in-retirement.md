The average Canadian retiree pays more tax than necessary — not because of bad luck, but because retirement tax planning is underrated. Income is more controllable in retirement than during your working years.

Here are 8 strategies that actually move the needle.

## 1. Pension Income Splitting

If you're 65 or older and receive eligible pension income (RRIF withdrawals, annuity income, defined benefit pension payments), you can split up to **50% of that income with your spouse** on your tax return.

The benefit: income shifts from the higher-earning spouse's return to the lower-earning spouse's return. If one spouse is in the 43% bracket and the other is in the 20% bracket, splitting $30,000 of RRIF income saves about $6,900/year in combined tax.

This is one of the few legitimate income-splitting mechanisms still available in Canada. Use it.

## 2. TFSA Withdrawals for High-Income Years

In years when your income from RRIF minimums, CPP, and OAS is pushing you toward higher brackets — draw from your TFSA instead of selling taxable investments. TFSA withdrawals are completely excluded from net income calculations, meaning they:

- Don't push you into higher brackets
- Don't trigger the OAS clawback
- Don't reduce income-tested benefits like the Guaranteed Income Supplement

## 3. RRSP Meltdown Before 71

As described in our RRSP meltdown article, drawing down your RRSP during the low-income years before CPP, OAS, and RRIF all start simultaneously can reduce your lifetime tax burden significantly.

The window: ages 60–70 for most retirees, when income is low and RRSP withdrawals can often be done in the 20–26% marginal bracket rather than 33–43%.

## 4. Delay CPP to 70

A counterintuitive tax strategy: deferring CPP to 70 reduces your income in years 65–69, keeping those years in lower tax brackets. At 70, your CPP is 42% higher — but by then, your RRSP should be smaller (from meltdown withdrawals) and your RRIF minimums may be manageable.

The combined effect: lower income in your 60s (taxed less), higher CPP later (but from a lower RRIF base), and OAS at full value.

## 5. Crystallize Capital Gains in Low-Income Years

Capital gains in non-registered accounts are taxed at 50% inclusion — meaning $10,000 of capital gains adds $5,000 to your taxable income. In a year when your income is low (say, age 62, before CPP/OAS/RRIF), you can sell appreciated investments and pay tax at a low marginal rate.

Deferring those same sales to age 74 when RRIF minimums have pushed you into the 40%+ bracket doubles your effective capital gains tax rate.

## 6. Claim the Age Amount and Pension Income Credits

At 65, two significant credits become available:

**Age Amount:** A federal credit worth up to $1,298/year (2025) for those with income below ~$99,000. Starts phasing out above $42,335.

**Pension Income Amount:** A federal credit of up to $2,000 of eligible pension income, worth up to $300 in tax savings. Triggers at $2,000+ of RRIF or DB pension income.

These are automatic but easy to forget if you're doing your own taxes.

## 7. Spousal RRSP Contributions Before Retirement

If one spouse will have significantly higher retirement income than the other, contribute to a **spousal RRSP** during working years. After 3 full calendar years, the withdrawing spouse pays tax at their lower rate.

If you're in your late 50s and your spouse is younger and in a lower tax bracket, spousal RRSP contributions now mean lower combined taxes at withdrawal.

## 8. Coordinate RIF Withdrawals with Your Spouse

RRIF minimums are calculated on each spouse's RRIF independently. But there's flexibility in how much you withdraw above the minimum. In a given year, if one spouse has unusually high income (sold a property, received an inheritance), the other spouse can limit their RRIF withdrawal to the minimum while the higher-income spouse takes less discretionary income.

Over a long retirement, active coordination of RRIF withdrawals between spouses — in the context of pension splitting — can smooth taxable income and reduce bracket exposure.

## The Tax-Free Death Benefit

Note: RRSP/RRIF balances transferred to a surviving spouse are tax-deferred on death. The surviving spouse rolls the assets into their own RRIF. The full value is taxable only when the second spouse dies — which may be 20+ years after the first.

This "survivor rollover" is not a tax avoidance strategy, but understanding it affects estate planning and how aggressively to draw down RRSPs vs. other assets.

## The Bottom Line

Retirement tax planning is not about finding loopholes. It's about timing income recognition across decades in a way that stays in lower brackets. The strategies above are legal, available to everyone, and underused.

The window for many of them is narrower than people realize — RRSP meltdown requires low-income years you may not have forever. The time to plan is before retirement, not after.
