The image of retirement as a debt-free chapter is increasingly outdated. A 2023 Statistics Canada survey found that 42% of Canadians aged 55–64 carry some form of non-mortgage debt. Many retire with both a mortgage balance and other debts.

Here's how to think about it.

## Not All Debt Is Equal in Retirement

**High-interest consumer debt (credit cards, personal loans above 8%):** The most dangerous. A $20,000 credit card balance at 19.99% costs $4,000/year in interest — money that provides no return and erodes retirement income. Eliminate this before retiring if at all possible.

**Mortgage debt (3–6% current rates):** The math is more nuanced. A $200,000 remaining mortgage at 5.5% costs $11,000/year in interest. If your investments return 6–7%, the math barely favors the mortgage. But the psychological cost and income inflexibility of carrying a mortgage into retirement are real.

**HELOC debt (prime + 0.5–1%, currently 6.2–6.7%):** Flexible but variable rate. In a rate-rising environment, HELOC interest can balloon. Dangerous as a long-term retirement financing tool.

**Low-interest car loan (0–4.9%):** If the rate is below your investment return, carrying it rather than using investment cash to pay it off is mathematically rational. The decision is mostly psychological.

## The Income Flow Problem

The primary issue with debt in retirement isn't the interest rate — it's the mandatory payment schedule.

A $1,500/month mortgage payment requires your retirement income to cover it regardless of market conditions. If your income is $5,000/month, 30% is committed before living expenses. If a market downturn reduces your RRIF value, you can't easily reduce that payment.

Debt payments in retirement reduce your income flexibility at exactly the moment you need it most.

## Debt vs RRSP in the 5 Years Before Retirement

The common question: should I use excess cash to pay down debt or contribute to my RRSP?

The framework:
1. If the debt rate > expected after-tax RRSP return → pay down debt
2. If the RRSP deduction saves tax at a higher rate than the debt costs → RRSP first, apply refund to debt
3. TFSA vs. debt: if debt rate is above 4%, pay debt; if below, TFSA may be marginally better but the certainty of debt elimination wins for most people near retirement

## The Debt Cascade Strategy for the Last 5 Years

If you have multiple debts at retirement minus 5 years:

1. **List all debts by interest rate (highest to lowest)**
2. **Pay minimums on all, throw every extra dollar at the highest rate**
3. **When one is eliminated, redirect its payment to the next**
4. **Apply RRSP refunds to the mortgage as a lump-sum payment**
5. **Goal:** Enter retirement mortgage-free, with only manageable low-rate debt if anything

## If You're Already Retired With a Mortgage

Options:
- **Make lump-sum payments** — most Canadian mortgages allow 10–20% of original principal annually without penalty
- **Use non-registered investment liquidations strategically** — sell in years when income is low to minimize tax, pay down mortgage
- **Reverse mortgage** — for those 55+ with significant equity who need income, a reverse mortgage is an option, though expensive (high rates and erodes estate value)
- **Downsize** — if your home has significant equity, selling and moving to a lower-cost property eliminates the mortgage and adds to retirement savings

## The Psychological Component

The financial planning literature consistently shows that debt-free retirees report significantly higher life satisfaction and lower financial stress — even when the financial math of carrying debt was close to neutral.

The certainty of no mortgage payment, no creditor obligations, and no financial vulnerability creates a sense of security that is genuinely valuable.

For those with borderline math, the psychological benefit is often sufficient reason to prioritize debt elimination before retirement.
