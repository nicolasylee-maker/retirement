If you retire with a large RRSP, the government will eventually force you to convert it to a RRIF and take minimum withdrawals — taxed as income — whether you need the money or not. The RRSP meltdown strategy is a way to take control of that tax hit before Ottawa does it for you.

## The Problem With a Big RRSP

Your RRSP is a tax deferral vehicle, not a tax elimination vehicle. Every dollar you put in was tax-free going in, but every dollar coming out is taxed as regular income.

The mandatory RRIF conversion at age 71 means you must start drawing down your RRSP — and the minimum withdrawal percentages increase every year. At 72, you must withdraw 5.28% of the RRIF value. By 80, that's 6.82%. By 90, it's 11.92%.

If your RRSP has grown to $600,000 by 71, you're looking at mandatory taxable income of $31,680 in year one — before CPP, OAS, or any other income. Combine that with CPP at $1,000/month ($12,000/year) and OAS at $727/month ($8,724/year), and you're at $52,404 just from those three sources. In Ontario, that puts you well into the 43% marginal bracket.

The RRSP meltdown is how you prevent that.

## How the Meltdown Works

The strategy: **withdraw from your RRSP in the years between retirement and mandatory RRIF conversion**, when your income is lower.

If you retire at 60 and defer CPP and OAS to 68, you have an 8-year window of relatively low income. In that window, you can draw $40–60K/year from your RRSP, pay tax at a low marginal rate (often 20–29%), and shift that money into your TFSA.

By 71, your RRSP is smaller. Your RRIF minimums are smaller. Your taxable income in retirement is permanently lower.

## The Numbers

Let's say you have $500,000 in your RRSP at age 60, earning 5%/year.

**Without meltdown:** At 71, RRSP grows to ~$862,000. Minimum withdrawal: $45,500. Combined with CPP + OAS, you're in the 43%+ bracket for most of your retirement.

**With meltdown:** You withdraw $50,000/year from 60–70. You pay tax at roughly 25% (after basic personal amount and marginal rates). Total tax on meltdown withdrawals: ~$125,000 over 10 years. At 71, RRSP balance: ~$350,000. Minimum withdrawal: ~$18,500. Combined with CPP + OAS, your marginal rate is 10–20% lower in your 70s and 80s.

Net tax savings: Often $50,000–$150,000 over a full retirement, depending on RRSP size and income sources.

## Where Does the Meltdown Money Go?

The withdrawn funds, after tax, should go into your **TFSA** if you have room. This converts taxable RRSP assets into tax-free TFSA assets — future growth and withdrawals from the TFSA don't count as income at all.

If your TFSA is full, a non-registered (taxable) investment account is the next best option. Capital gains are taxed at 50% inclusion rate, which is better than RRSP withdrawals taxed at 100% inclusion.

## Drawbacks to Consider

**You pay tax now instead of later.** If your income in retirement ends up lower than expected (lower CPP, large medical expenses, etc.), the meltdown might not save as much as projected.

**TFSA room is finite.** As of 2025, cumulative contribution room is $95,000. If your RRSP meltdown produces more after-tax cash than your TFSA can absorb, some goes into taxable accounts with lower tax efficiency.

**Investment returns affect the math.** A higher return on the RRSP makes the meltdown less effective (more growth to draw down), while a lower return makes it more effective.

## Who Should Consider It

The meltdown works best for:
- Early retirees (before 65) with significant RRSP savings
- People with defined benefit pensions who will have high forced income at 71+
- People delaying CPP and OAS (creates the low-income window)
- Those with available TFSA room to absorb withdrawals tax-free

It works less well for:
- People who retire with modest RRSPs (the forced RRIF minimums won't be painful anyway)
- Those who need all their RRSP for living expenses (no choice — but no tax optimization either)

## The Bottom Line

The RRSP meltdown is one of the highest-leverage tax strategies available to Canadian retirees. Done right, it converts high-tax future income into lower-tax current income and shifts assets into your tax-free TFSA.

Start the analysis at least 10 years before 71. The earlier you plan, the more flexibility you have.
