The 4% rule is the most famous number in retirement planning. Withdraw 4% of your portfolio in year one, adjust for inflation annually, and your money will last 30 years — based on historical US market returns.

But you're Canadian. Does it still apply?

## Where the 4% Rule Came From

William Bengen's 1994 study examined every 30-year retirement window from 1926 to 1994. He found that a 50/50 stock/bond portfolio could sustain a 4% initial withdrawal rate through every historical period, including the Great Depression and 1970s stagflation.

The research was refined into the "Trinity Study" in 1998. Based on US large-cap stock and intermediate bond returns, a 4% withdrawal rate had a 95%+ success rate over 30 years.

## The Canadian Market Differences

Canada's stock market is heavily concentrated in financials, energy, and materials — about 60% of the TSX. The diversification in US studies was broader.

However, Canadian equities have historically had similar long-run real returns to US equities. The bigger issues for Canadians aren't market returns — they're taxes and income sources.

## Why Taxes Change the Math

The 4% rule was derived on pre-tax withdrawals. In Canada, RRSP/RRIF withdrawals are fully taxable.

If you need $60,000/year in after-tax income and your marginal rate is 33%, you need to withdraw ~$89,552 gross to net $60,000. Your real withdrawal rate is 8.95% on a $1,000,000 portfolio — not 4%.

The solution: plan withdrawal rates in after-tax terms, account for the tax on RRSP/RRIF withdrawals, and use TFSA withdrawals (no tax) to your advantage.

## How CPP and OAS Change the Required Withdrawal Rate

This is where Canadian retirees are actually better off than the American model assumes.

CPP and OAS provide guaranteed, inflation-indexed income that reduces portfolio dependence. If you receive $30,000/year from CPP + OAS and need $70,000/year to live, your portfolio only needs to produce $40,000/year.

If your portfolio is $1,000,000, that's a 4% withdrawal rate on the full portfolio — but you're only taking $40,000, a 4% rate on the true gap.

Effectively, high CPP + OAS income makes your portfolio's "real" withdrawal rate lower than 4%, which improves long-term sustainability.

**Canadians who maximize CPP and OAS (especially by deferring to 70) can often sustain a lower-than-4% withdrawal rate from their portfolio indefinitely.**

## The 30-Year Assumption

The 4% rule targets a 30-year horizon (retirement at 65 to 95). If you retire at 55, you need a 40-year horizon. Bengen's data suggests a lower safe withdrawal rate of ~3.5% for 40-year periods.

If you retire at 70 and only need a 25-year horizon, 4.5–5% becomes defensible.

## A Canadian Framework for Withdrawal Rate Planning

Rather than applying a single rule, consider this framework:

1. **Calculate your guaranteed income floor:** CPP + OAS + defined benefit pension
2. **Calculate your annual gap:** Spending minus guaranteed income
3. **Apply the withdrawal rate to the gap, not your total assets:** If your gap is $30,000 and your portfolio is $900,000, your withdrawal rate is 3.3% — very sustainable
4. **Adjust for time horizon:** 30-year horizon → 4%. 40-year → 3.5%. 25-year → 4.5%
5. **Account for tax:** Use after-tax withdrawal amounts when modeling

## The Dynamic Withdrawal Alternative

A fixed 4% withdrawal adjusted for inflation regardless of portfolio performance is actually not what most financial planners recommend today. Dynamic withdrawal strategies — reducing withdrawals 10–15% in bad years, increasing in good years — significantly improve long-term sustainability.

A retiree who can reduce spending from $70,000 to $62,000 in a bad market year (drawing more from TFSA, cutting discretionary) can sustain a portfolio that would otherwise fail under a rigid 4% rule.

## The Bottom Line

The 4% rule is a reasonable starting point for Canadians, but it requires adjustment for:
- After-tax withdrawal amounts (higher gross needed)
- CPP/OAS as income offset (lower net needed from portfolio)
- Time horizon adjustments
- The flexibility of your spending

For most Canadians with average CPP and full OAS, the effective safe withdrawal rate from their portfolio — after accounting for guaranteed income — is in the 3–4% range. With deferred CPP/OAS and a well-structured withdrawal strategy, some retirees find they barely need to draw on their portfolio at all.
