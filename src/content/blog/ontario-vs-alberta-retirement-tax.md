# Ontario vs Alberta Retirement Taxes: A Side-by-Side Comparison

Alberta has no provincial sales tax and the lowest provincial income tax in Canada. Ontario has a higher income tax rate, a provincial sales tax (HST), and is home to most Canadians. So how much do you actually save by retiring in Alberta versus Ontario — and does it justify a move?

## Provincial Income Tax Rates (2025)

**Alberta's provincial income tax:**
- 10% on the first $148,269
- 12% on $148,269–$177,922
- 13%, 14%, 15% on higher income

For retirees drawing $50,000–$100,000/year, virtually all income falls in the **10% bracket**.

**Ontario's provincial income tax:**
- 5.05% on the first $51,446
- 9.15% on $51,446–$102,894
- 11.16% on $102,894–$150,000
- 12.16% on $150,000–$220,000

For retirees drawing $50,000–$100,000, income spans the **5.05% and 9.15% brackets**.

## Side-by-Side Tax Comparison

Assumptions: Single retiree, all income from RRIF/CPP/OAS, no pension income splitting (to keep it simple). Using 2025 rates including federal tax.

**At $50,000 total retirement income:**

| Tax Component | Ontario | Alberta |
|---|---|---|
| Federal tax | ~$6,800 | ~$6,800 |
| Provincial income tax | ~$2,300 | ~$2,100 |
| **Total income tax** | **~$9,100** | **~$8,900** |
| HST on spending (~$30K spent) | ~$2,400 | $0 |
| **Total tax burden** | **~$11,500** | **~$8,900** |

**Annual saving in Alberta: ~$2,600**

**At $80,000 total retirement income:**

| Tax Component | Ontario | Alberta |
|---|---|---|
| Federal tax | ~$14,200 | ~$14,200 |
| Provincial income tax | ~$6,100 | ~$4,800 |
| **Total income tax** | **~$20,300** | **~$19,000** |
| HST on spending (~$45K spent) | ~$3,600 | $0 |
| **Total tax burden** | **~$23,900** | **~$19,000** |

**Annual saving in Alberta: ~$4,900**

**At $120,000 total retirement income:**

| Tax Component | Ontario | Alberta |
|---|---|---|
| Federal tax | ~$25,800 | ~$25,800 |
| Provincial income tax | ~$12,600 | ~$8,000 |
| **Total income tax** | **~$38,400** | **~$33,800** |
| HST on spending (~$60K spent) | ~$4,800 | $0 |
| **Total tax burden** | **~$43,200** | **~$33,800** |

**Annual saving in Alberta: ~$9,400**

*Note: HST savings are approximate. Alberta has GST (5%) on many purchases; Ontario charges HST (13%), so the net provincial sales tax difference is 8% of taxable spending.*

## What About Property Taxes?

Property taxes are the third major tax comparison. Here's the rough picture for comparable homes:

**Toronto/GTA:**
- Average property tax rate: ~0.6–0.7% of assessed value
- $900,000 home: ~$5,400–$6,300/year

**Ottawa:**
- Average rate: ~1.1%
- $600,000 home: ~$6,600/year

**Calgary:**
- Average rate: ~0.67%
- $700,000 home: ~$4,700/year

**Edmonton:**
- Average rate: ~0.87%
- $500,000 home: ~$4,350/year

Property tax advantage is modest and depends heavily on the specific municipality and home value. Alberta cities aren't dramatically lower than Ontario cities on property tax.

## What the Tax Savings Don't Tell You

**Housing cost differential:** The average detached home in the GTA is $1.1M–$1.3M. In Calgary, similar homes run $700,000–$900,000. The difference ($300,000–$500,000) earning 4% generates $12,000–$20,000/year — a bigger financial benefit than the annual tax savings for most people.

But that $400,000 difference requires actually selling your Ontario home and buying an Alberta one. Many retirees have children and grandchildren in Ontario, making this emotionally complicated.

**Healthcare:** Both provinces provide universal healthcare. Wait times are comparable. Alberta has no provincial seniors' drug benefit as comprehensive as Ontario's ODB program — Ontario's ODB covers many prescription drugs for seniors 65+ with modest co-pays, which can be worth $1,000–$3,000/year for those on multiple medications.

**GIS and low-income supplements:** For retirees with modest incomes, Ontario's GAINS (Guaranteed Annual Income System) adds up to $166/month on top of federal GIS. Alberta has no equivalent provincial GIS top-up. For lower-income retirees, Ontario may actually be more generous net of all programs.

## Ontario-Specific Tax Credits for Seniors

Ontario offers several senior-specific credits that partially offset higher taxes:

**Ontario Senior Homeowners' Property Tax Grant:** Up to $500/year for qualifying low-income homeowners 64+.

**Ontario Property and Energy Tax Credit:** Can reduce net provincial taxes for lower-income seniors.

**GAINS supplement:** Up to $166/month for low-income seniors on GIS, with no Alberta equivalent.

These credits don't eliminate the Ontario-Alberta gap but do reduce it for moderate-income retirees.

## Who Benefits Most from Moving to Alberta?

The tax savings grow with income. The math clearly favours Alberta for retirees with:
- **$80,000+/year in retirement income** — annual savings of $4,900–$9,400
- **No compelling reason to stay in Ontario** (no family, no existing property)
- **High discretionary spending** where HST savings compound
- **Strong real estate in Ontario** — selling at peak Ontario prices and buying in Alberta compounds the advantage

The math is less compelling for:
- **Lower-income retirees ($40,000–$60,000)** — savings of $1,500–$2,500/year may not justify moving away from family and support networks
- **Retirees on multiple medications** who value Ontario's ODB program
- **Those with family in Ontario** — the emotional and travel costs of distance are real

## Verdict

At modest retirement incomes ($50,000–$70,000), Alberta saves roughly $2,000–$3,500/year in combined income and sales taxes. Over 25 years, that's $50,000–$87,000 — not trivial, but not a life-changing sum either.

At higher incomes ($80,000–$120,000), the gap widens to $5,000–$9,000/year — $125,000–$225,000 over 25 years. That's meaningful.

For most retirees, the decision ultimately hinges on family proximity and lifestyle preferences, not taxes. But if you're on the fence and financially motivated, the numbers clearly favour Alberta.
