# Retiring Abroad as a Canadian: Taxes, CPP, OAS, and What to Plan For

Portugal, Mexico, Costa Rica, Spain, Thailand — Canada's retirees increasingly look beyond borders for lower costs of living, warmer climates, and new adventures. But retiring abroad introduces significant tax and benefit complexity that domestic planners often miss.

## Will You Still Get CPP and OAS?

**CPP:** Yes, unconditionally. CPP is a contributory pension tied to your work history, not your residency. You receive CPP regardless of where you live in the world. Payments can be deposited to a Canadian bank account or transferred internationally.

**OAS:** More complex. OAS is available to non-residents, but:
- You must have lived in Canada for at least 40 years after age 18 for the full pension
- Partial OAS is available if you've lived in Canada for at least 20 years after age 18
- Payments continue to non-residents, but **withholding tax applies**

**OAS non-resident withholding tax:** By default, Canada withholds 25% of OAS payments to non-residents. However, most countries have tax treaties with Canada that reduce this to 15%. Check whether your destination country has a Canada tax treaty.

**GIS (Guaranteed Income Supplement):** This requires residency in Canada. If you leave, GIS stops immediately. Non-residents are ineligible.

## CPP and OAS Withholding Tax

**CPP to non-residents:** Subject to non-resident withholding tax. Default rate is 25%, reduced by tax treaty. The US treaty caps it at 15%. Many popular retirement destinations (Portugal, Mexico, Costa Rica, Thailand) have treaties capping withholding at 15%.

**Application for treaty rate:** File an NR5 form with the CRA to apply the treaty rate. Without the form, Canada deducts at the full 25%.

**Reporting in your new country:** Most countries require you to report foreign income (including CPP/OAS) and may provide a credit for Canadian taxes withheld to avoid double taxation.

## Canadian Residency and Tax Status

When you leave Canada to retire abroad, you have a choice:

**Option 1: Become a non-resident of Canada**

If you sever residential ties (sell or rent your home, cancel provincial health insurance, move financial relationships), you're generally a non-resident after departure.

Non-resident status means:
- You pay Canadian tax only on Canadian-source income (CPP, OAS, RRIF withdrawals, rental income from Canadian properties)
- You file an NR4 or NR6 (non-resident tax forms), not a regular T1
- Withholding tax replaces regular tax filing for many income types
- RRSP/RRIF withdrawals are subject to 25% withholding (or treaty rate)
- TFSA: You can no longer contribute as a non-resident, and room does not accumulate. The account balance can stay — earnings remain tax-free in Canada (though taxable in some foreign countries)

**Option 2: Remain a Canadian resident**

If you maintain significant ties (home in Canada, spouse in Canada, provincial health insurance), CRA may deem you still a Canadian resident. You'd file a regular T1 return and pay Canadian income tax on worldwide income.

For most dedicated international retirees, severing ties and becoming a non-resident is cleaner and often tax-advantageous.

## The Departure Tax

When you leave Canada as a non-resident, you're deemed to have sold your assets at fair market value on departure day — a **deemed disposition**. This triggers capital gains on:
- Non-registered investments (stocks, mutual funds)
- Foreign real estate
- Business interests

**Exceptions:** RRSP, RRIF, TFSA, and Canadian real estate are exempt from departure tax (real estate is exempt because Canada retains taxing rights when you eventually sell).

Plan the departure year carefully. A large capital gain from deemed disposition could be partially offset by RRSP contributions (if you still have room and earned income), or timed to a low-income year.

## Healthcare: The Critical Planning Gap

This is where most Canadians are unprepared. Provincial health insurance (OHIP, BCMSP, etc.) typically cancels after 7 months outside the province. Once non-resident, you have no Canadian healthcare coverage.

**Options:**
- Purchase private international health insurance (essential for any long-term expat)
- Enroll in your new country's public healthcare system (if available — Portugal, Spain, and many other countries have reciprocal health agreements or allow residents to enroll)
- Maintain a hybrid: private international insurance + coverage in your new country

Private expat health insurance for a 65-year-old costs approximately $3,000–$8,000/year depending on coverage level and destination. This is a real cost that must factor into your retirement income planning.

## Popular Destinations for Canadian Retirees

**Portugal (Non-Habitual Resident Program):** Portugal's NHR tax regime (now restructured as IFICI for new applicants in 2024+) historically offered favourable tax treatment on foreign income. Many Canadians have retired there. Healthcare is reasonably accessible and affordable.

**Mexico:** Low cost of living, warm climate, close to Canada. Canada-Mexico tax treaty limits withholding to 15%. Healthcare is lower cost; private insurance widely available.

**Costa Rica:** "Pensionado" visa available to those receiving at least US$1,000/month in pension income. No tax on foreign-source income for residents. Popular expat community.

**Spain:** Canada-Spain tax treaty. EU healthcare for residents. Higher cost than Mexico but excellent quality of life. Spain's Beckham Law may have tax benefits depending on income type.

**Thailand:** No Canada-Thailand tax treaty — full 25% withholding applies unless arranged otherwise. Lower cost of living but no treaty protection.

## Financial Logistics

**Keep a Canadian bank account.** You need it to receive CPP/OAS deposits and manage Canadian financial affairs. Most Canadian banks allow non-residents to maintain accounts.

**TFSA.** You can no longer contribute as a non-resident (1% monthly penalty tax if you do), but you don't have to close it. The balance continues to grow tax-free in Canada. Some countries tax TFSA income — check your destination's tax rules on Canadian accounts.

**RRSP/RRIF as a non-resident.** RRIF minimum withdrawals continue. Withholding tax applies. For lump-sum withdrawals, consider timing: withdrawing all at once is subject to withholding, but your total Canadian tax return may generate a refund if total income is below a threshold (this depends on treaty terms).

**Currency risk.** If your Canadian pension income is fixed in CAD and your expenses are in a foreign currency, exchange rate shifts significantly affect your purchasing power. Consider holding some savings in USD or EUR.

## The Six-Month Rule for Snowbirds

Many Canadians want to spend winters in warmer countries (US, Mexico, Caribbean) while maintaining Canadian residency. The key constraint: **the US allows Canadian visitors for up to 6 months per year**. Staying longer triggers US substantial presence tax rules.

For maintaining Canadian residency while snowbirding:
- Keep provincial health insurance active (7 months outside the province is usually the limit)
- Maintain your Canadian home
- File Canadian tax returns as a resident

This "snowbird" approach lets you enjoy warmer winters without severing Canadian ties — and preserves GIS eligibility, provincial drug benefits, and full healthcare coverage when in Canada.

## Key Action Items Before Leaving

1. Consult a cross-border tax specialist (not just a regular accountant)
2. File an NR5 to establish treaty withholding rates on CPP/OAS
3. Purchase expat health insurance before provincial coverage lapses
4. Decide on RRSP/RRIF strategy (drawdown before departure to reduce deemed disposition complexity? Or carry it as a non-resident?)
5. Understand your destination country's tax obligations
6. Keep your Canadian banking relationships active

Retiring abroad is increasingly feasible for Canadians — the combination of CPP, OAS, and modest RRIF income goes far in many countries. But the planning is meaningfully more complex than retiring domestically. Get the tax advice right before you go.
