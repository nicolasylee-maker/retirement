Old Age Security pays up to $727/month at 65 — but if your income crosses a certain threshold, the government starts clawing it back. At high enough income, you lose every dollar.

Most Canadians don't realize this until they retire and see a chunk of their OAS disappear. Here's how it works, the exact numbers, and what you can do about it.

## What Is the OAS Clawback?

The OAS clawback — officially called the "OAS recovery tax" — is a 15-cent reduction for every dollar of net income above the threshold. For 2025, that threshold is **$90,997**.

If your net income is $100,000, you repay 15% of the excess ($9,003), which is $1,350/year — about $113/month off your OAS.

At net income of roughly **$148,000**, your OAS is completely eliminated.

## The Clawback Income Threshold (2025)

| Net Income | Annual OAS Lost | Monthly OAS Lost |
|------------|----------------|-----------------|
| $90,997 | $0 | $0 |
| $100,000 | $1,350 | $113 |
| $110,000 | $2,850 | $238 |
| $120,000 | $4,350 | $363 |
| $130,000 | $5,850 | $488 |
| $140,000 | $7,350 | $613 |
| $148,451 | $8,727 | $727 (full OAS) |

These thresholds are indexed to inflation and rise slightly each year.

## What Counts as Income for the Clawback?

The clawback is based on your **net income** (line 23600 of your tax return), which includes:

- CPP payments
- OAS itself (yes, OAS counts toward the clawback threshold)
- RRSP/RRIF withdrawals
- Pension income
- Investment income (dividends, interest, capital gains)
- Employment income
- Rental income

**What doesn't count:** TFSA withdrawals. This is the most important planning tool available.

## Why This Matters More Than You Think

Most people focus on the income tax rate on their withdrawals. But the OAS clawback effectively adds 15 cents on the dollar to your marginal tax rate above the threshold.

In Ontario, if you're in the 43.41% marginal bracket at $100,000 income, the clawback pushes your effective marginal rate to **58.41%** on income between $90,997 and the full clawback point. That's a brutal rate.

## How to Minimize the OAS Clawback

### 1. Melt Down Your RRSP Before 65

If you retire at 60–65, consider drawing down your RRSP before OAS begins. You'll pay tax on the withdrawals, but at a lower rate than if you're also collecting CPP + OAS + RRIF minimums later.

The strategy: take $40–60K/year from your RRSP in your early retirement years, keeping income below the OAS threshold. This reduces the RRIF minimum withdrawals you'll face at 72+.

### 2. Use TFSA Withdrawals in High-Income Years

TFSA withdrawals don't count as income. In years when your RRIF minimums, CPP, and OAS push you near or above the clawback threshold, draw from your TFSA instead of selling taxable investments.

### 3. Pension Income Splitting

If you have a pension or RRIF, you can split up to 50% of that income with your spouse. If your spouse is in a lower tax bracket, this can pull your individual net income below the clawback threshold while theirs stays well under it.

### 4. Delay OAS to 70

If your income will be above the clawback threshold between 65 and 70, consider delaying OAS. You gain 36% more per month, and by the time you collect, your RRIF withdrawals may have declined enough to keep you under the threshold.

### 5. Defer Capital Gains

If you hold investments in a taxable account, timing large capital gains realizations matters. Selling in a year with lower other income avoids triggering the clawback.

## The TFSA Is Your Best Tool

Every dollar in your TFSA is a dollar that won't trigger the OAS clawback — ever. This is why maximizing TFSA contributions in your 50s is one of the highest-leverage retirement planning moves available to Canadians.

At current contribution room of $95,000 (2025, if you've never contributed), a fully-funded TFSA growing at 5% for 10 years would be worth ~$155,000 — producing tax-free withdrawals that don't affect your OAS one cent.

## Ontario-Specific Note

Ontario provincial tax doesn't have a separate OAS clawback, but the federal clawback applies to all provinces. The effect is most painful in Ontario because the combined federal-Ontario marginal rates are already among the highest in Canada.

If you're in Ontario with significant RRIF income, the effective marginal rate above $90,997 is worth modeling carefully before choosing your withdrawal strategy.

## The Bottom Line

The OAS clawback is a hidden retirement tax that hits the people who planned best — those with large RRSPs and pensions. The antidotes are TFSA maximization, RRSP meltdown before 65, pension splitting, and income smoothing across retirement years.

The good news: with planning, most Canadians can keep their net income below or near the clawback threshold. The earlier you model this, the more flexibility you have.
