Accumulating wealth is one skill. Turning that wealth into reliable income is a different skill — and one that most Canadians have never had to practice.

Here's a framework for building a retirement income strategy that minimizes tax, handles uncertainty, and supports flexible spending.

## The Income Sources Stack

Think of retirement income as a layered stack, from most guaranteed to least:

**Layer 1: Fully guaranteed (indexed)**
- CPP
- OAS
- Defined benefit pension

**Layer 2: Quasi-guaranteed (depleting)**
- RRIF minimum withdrawals (forced but amount declines with balance)
- Annuity payments (fixed, not depleting)

**Layer 3: Flexible (as needed)**
- TFSA withdrawals
- Non-registered investment withdrawals (capital gains taxed)
- RRIF withdrawals above minimum

**Layer 4: Reserve (emergency/irregular)**
- HELOC (home equity, if needed)
- Investment liquidation

The goal: cover your fixed living expenses with Layers 1–2 and use Layers 3–4 for discretionary spending, healthcare events, and market volatility buffers.

## Start With the Gap

Map your annual spending against your Layer 1 income:

1. Annual spending target: $X
2. Minus: CPP + OAS + pension = $Y
3. Gap = $X – $Y

This gap is what your portfolio must cover. Everything flows from this number.

## The Account Drawdown Sequence

The tax-optimal sequence for most Canadian retirees:

**In most years:**
1. Take mandatory RRIF minimums (forced, unavoidable)
2. Supplement with TFSA as needed (tax-free, no OAS clawback impact)
3. If more income needed: take additional RRIF above minimum

**In low-income years (below $42,000):**
- Consider topping up taxable income to $42,000 (age amount phase-out threshold)
- Use the space to make additional RRIF withdrawals at low marginal rates

**In high-income years (above $90,997):**
- Use TFSA exclusively to avoid OAS clawback
- Minimize RRIF discretionary withdrawals

## The TFSA as the Income Strategy Wildcard

The TFSA's superpower in a retirement income strategy is flexibility:

- In years with high RRIF income: use TFSA to supplement, don't push into higher brackets
- In years with unexpected expenses (medical, home repair): TFSA instead of selling investments at potentially bad prices
- In years when CPP/OAS temporarily exceeds spending: redirect to TFSA

The TFSA should be the last major account depleted, not the first. Its tax-free nature and flexibility become more valuable as retirement progresses and RRIF minimums increase.

## Timing CPP and OAS for Income Strategy Optimization

The optimal CPP/OAS start ages aren't just about breakeven analysis — they also affect your income strategy:

**Taking early (60–65):** Reduces portfolio dependence immediately but permanently locks in a lower payment. Less ability to defer to higher-income years.

**Deferring to 70:** Creates a low-income window from 60–70 where RRSP meltdown can occur at lower tax rates. At 70, CPP + OAS provide a large, indexed income base that reduces portfolio dependence for the remainder of life.

For most people in good health with adequate savings to bridge to 70: defer both CPP and OAS to 70. Use the bridge years for RRSP meltdown.

## Managing Income Volatility

Retirement income has natural volatility from:
- RRIF value changes (affects future minimums)
- Investment return variation
- Healthcare expenses
- Property taxes and maintenance

Strategies to smooth income:
- **Cash cushion:** 12–24 months of living expenses in savings/GICs, not invested in equities
- **Flexible spending range:** Define a "comfortable" and "acceptable" spending range — you can tighten in bad years, relax in good years
- **Annual review:** Reassess CPP deferral, RRIF withdrawal amounts, and TFSA allocation once a year

## The Annual Retirement Income Review

Each January, review:
1. RRIF balance January 1 → calculate mandatory minimum withdrawal
2. CPP + OAS payments → confirm for the year
3. Expected total income → compare to spending plan and tax brackets
4. Adjust TFSA or additional RRIF withdrawals to optimize brackets
5. Check OAS clawback exposure

This annual calibration typically takes 1–2 hours and is the difference between optimized and unoptimized retirement tax.
