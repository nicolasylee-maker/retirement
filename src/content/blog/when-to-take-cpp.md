Canada Pension Plan gives you a choice: start as early as age 60, or wait as late as age 70. Every year you delay past 65 adds **8.4% more per year** to your monthly payment — permanently. Every year you take it early past 65 reduces it by **7.2% per year**.

That's a wide range. And the "right" answer depends entirely on your health, your other income sources, and how long you expect to live.

Here's the complete math — and how to think through it.

## The Numbers: What Each Start Age Pays

The government's standard CPP retirement benefit at age 65 in 2025 is **$1,364/month** (the maximum). Most Canadians get less — the average is around $780/month — but let's use $1,000/month at 65 as a clean baseline.

| Start Age | Adjustment | Monthly Payment | Annual Payment |
|-----------|-----------|----------------|----------------|
| 60 | −36% | $640/mo | $7,680/yr |
| 61 | −28.8% | $712/mo | $8,544/yr |
| 62 | −21.6% | $784/mo | $9,408/yr |
| 63 | −14.4% | $856/mo | $10,272/yr |
| 64 | −7.2% | $928/mo | $11,136/yr |
| **65** | **0%** | **$1,000/mo** | **$12,000/yr** |
| 66 | +8.4% | $1,084/mo | $13,008/yr |
| 67 | +16.8% | $1,168/mo | $14,016/yr |
| 68 | +25.2% | $1,252/mo | $15,024/yr |
| 69 | +33.6% | $1,336/mo | $16,032/yr |
| 70 | +42% | $1,420/mo | $17,040/yr |

Taking CPP at 60 versus 70 is a difference of **$780/month — for life**.

## The Breakeven Ages

"Breakeven" means the age at which delaying CPP pays off more in total lifetime income than taking it early.

**Taking at 60 vs. 65:**
You collect 5 extra years of $640/month = $38,400 head start. But you permanently give up $360/month. The breakeven is around **age 74**. After 74, taking CPP at 65 pays more in total.

**Taking at 65 vs. 70:**
You give up 5 years of $1,000/month = $60,000. But you gain $420/month permanently. The breakeven is around **age 82–83**. After 83, the age-70 strategy wins.

**The key question: Do you expect to live past the breakeven?**

The average 65-year-old Canadian man lives to **84**. The average woman lives to **87**. If you're in good health, the odds favour delaying.

## The Case for Taking CPP Early (Age 60–64)

Taking CPP early makes sense when:

- **You need the income now.** If you're retired early and drawing down savings, CPP at 60 reduces the portfolio withdrawal — which preserves more for later.
- **Your health is poor.** If you have reason to believe your life expectancy is below average, front-loading income is rational.
- **You have a shorter investing horizon.** If you would otherwise park the early CPP in a savings account, the math shifts. But if you'd invest it and earn 5%+ annually, the delayed option still usually wins on a risk-adjusted basis.
- **You're still working.** CPP taken before 65 while still working has some complications, but it can make sense if you're winding down employment income.

## The Case for Delaying CPP (Age 66–70)

Delaying is usually the right move when:

- **You have other income sources to bridge the gap.** RRSP withdrawals, TFSA drawdowns, or pension income can cover ages 65–70 while you wait.
- **You're in good health.** Every year past 83 that you live, the age-70 strategy pays off more.
- **You want to reduce longevity risk.** CPP is inflation-indexed and guaranteed for life. The bigger the CPP cheque, the less you depend on your portfolio surviving to age 90+.
- **You're concerned about OAS clawback.** Larger CPP payments can trigger the OAS clawback if your income exceeds ~$90,000. But this is rarely a reason *not* to delay — it's just a planning consideration.

## The RRSP Meltdown Strategy Connection

One popular strategy: **melt down your RRSP from ages 65–71** (before mandatory RRIF conversion), while delaying CPP to 70.

The logic:
1. Pull $40–60K/year from RRSP at relatively low tax rates (before CPP and OAS pile on)
2. Delay CPP to 70 for the maximum guaranteed payment
3. At 70+, live primarily on CPP + OAS + TFSA — portfolio survives longer

This strategy works especially well if you have a large RRSP and a small TFSA, because it reduces your future RRIF minimums (which are taxed as income) and shifts the tax hit to years when your marginal rate is lower.

## The Inflation Protection Factor

CPP is indexed to the Consumer Price Index (CPI) every January. A $1,000/month payment today is worth ~$1,280/month in 10 years at 2.5% inflation.

This matters for the breakeven calculation. The $780/month difference between taking CPP at 60 vs. 70 grows every year with inflation. In 20 years, that difference is closer to $1,270/month in today's dollars.

The longer you live, the more valuable the inflation protection on a higher base payment becomes.

## The Short Answer

**If you're in average or better health and can afford to wait:** delay CPP to at least 65, ideally 67–70.

**If you have health concerns or need the income now:** take it earlier and invest it if possible.

**Never make the decision in isolation.** Run the numbers with your full financial picture — RRSP, TFSA, other pensions, and your expected spending — before deciding.
