When you leave a job with a defined contribution pension or a defined benefit pension before retirement, your pension entitlement is often transferred to a Locked-In Retirement Account (LIRA). Eventually, that LIRA converts to a Life Income Fund (LIF). A LIF is similar to a RRIF but with important restrictions.

## What Is a LIRA?

A Locked-In Retirement Account holds pension money that you've earned but haven't yet started receiving. It's "locked in" because the original pension funds were specifically designated for retirement income by the plan sponsor and legislation.

You can't make new contributions to a LIRA and — unlike an RRSP — you can't withdraw freely. The purpose is to preserve pension assets for retirement income.

LIRAs grow tax-sheltered, just like RRSPs.

## Converting a LIRA to a LIF

By the end of the year you turn 71 (same as RRSP/RRIF), you must convert your LIRA to a Life Income Fund, a life annuity, or a variable benefit.

The LIF is the most common choice — it's similar to a RRIF but with one critical difference: a maximum withdrawal limit.

## LIF Minimum and Maximum Withdrawals

**Minimum:** Same as RRIF minimums, based on age. You must withdraw at least this amount each year.

**Maximum:** Also set by provincial legislation. The maximum is designed to prevent withdrawing the entire fund early, preserving the funds for lifetime income purposes.

In Ontario, the maximum LIF withdrawal percentage is higher than the minimum but limits you from withdrawing the entire balance in early years.

This double constraint (minimum and maximum) is the defining characteristic of a LIF vs. a RRIF. With a RRIF, you can withdraw as much as you want above the minimum. With a LIF, you cannot.

## The Unlocking Provisions

Most provinces allow some degree of "unlocking" — accessing LIF funds beyond the normal maximum — under specific circumstances:

**Ontario unlocking options:**

1. **50% Unlocking at 55+:** A one-time option to transfer 50% of your LIRA/LIF balance to an RRSP or RRIF, removing the locked-in restriction on that portion.

2. **Small amounts:** If the LIF balance is below a threshold ($29,285 in 2025 in Ontario), you can cash it out entirely.

3. **Non-residency:** Canadians who have been non-residents for 2+ years can unlock pension funds.

4. **Shortened life expectancy:** Medical certification of shortened life expectancy allows full unlocking.

5. **Low income:** In some provinces (not Ontario), low income qualifies for unlocking.

The 50% unlocking option is the most commonly used. It takes the locked-in restriction off half your LIF balance and rolls it into a RRIF where you have full withdrawal flexibility.

## LIF vs RRIF: Key Differences

| Feature | RRIF | LIF |
|---------|------|-----|
| Minimum withdrawals | Yes | Yes |
| Maximum withdrawals | No | Yes |
| Lump sum withdrawal | Anytime | Limited/restricted |
| New contributions | No | No |
| Source of funds | RRSP | Pension/LIRA |
| Eligible for pension income credit | Yes (65+) | Yes (65+) |
| Eligible for pension income splitting | Yes (65+) | Yes (65+) |

## Planning Implications

If you have a significant LIF balance:

1. **Model the maximum withdrawal constraint.** Unlike a RRIF, you may not be able to take a large one-time withdrawal in a specific year.

2. **Consider the 50% unlocking option at 55.** Transferring 50% to a RRIF gives you more flexibility for RRSP meltdown strategies and large discretionary withdrawals.

3. **Name your spouse as successor annuitant.** On death, the LIF converts to the surviving spouse's LIF — tax-deferred, same locked-in rules.
