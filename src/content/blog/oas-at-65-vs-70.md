Old Age Security can be deferred beyond 65, just like CPP. Every month you delay past 65 adds 0.6% to your monthly benefit — up to a maximum of 36% more at age 70.

For 2025, the maximum OAS at 65 is $727.67/month. At 70, it's $989.63/month.

That's $262/month more — for life — if you wait 5 years.

## The Breakeven Analysis

You give up 60 months × $727.67 = $43,660 to delay to 70. You gain $261.96/month more permanently.

Breakeven: $43,660 ÷ $261.96 = **167 months** (about 13.9 years after age 70 = age 83.9).

If you live past 84, delaying OAS to 70 pays more in total lifetime income. The average 65-year-old Canadian woman lives to 87. The average man to 84.

## When Delaying OAS Makes Sense

**You're in good health** with family longevity. Every year past 84 the delayed strategy wins more.

**You're doing an RRSP meltdown.** If you're drawing down your RRSP from 65–70, your income is deliberately low — you don't need the OAS income. Let it grow.

**You're near the OAS clawback threshold.** If your income between 65–70 will be near or above $90,997, you'll clawback much of the OAS anyway. Delaying avoids the clawback on those years and builds a larger benefit for your lower-income 70s.

**Your spouse is younger.** OAS is individual. If your spouse is several years younger, their financial picture may be better served by you taking less income now.

## When Taking OAS at 65 Makes Sense

**Health concerns.** If you have reason to believe your life expectancy is below average, the breakeven at 84 is too far away.

**You need the income.** If your retirement plan requires OAS income at 65 to cover expenses, deferring is a luxury you can't afford.

**Your income at 65–70 is low.** If you're in a low bracket during those years anyway, there's no clawback issue, and the income is genuinely useful.

**You want to reduce RRSP/TFSA drawdown.** The more guaranteed income you have now, the less your portfolio needs to produce and the longer it lasts.

## The CPP and OAS Deferral Combination

Deferring both CPP and OAS to 70 is the maximum guaranteed income strategy. The combined benefit at 70 for a single person at maximum entitlements: $1,364 (CPP) + $990 (OAS) = **$2,354/month**, indexed to inflation, for life.

That's $28,248/year of guaranteed, inflation-protected income — before any savings withdrawals. For most Canadians, this covers basic living expenses and dramatically reduces portfolio risk.

The bridge: from retirement to 70, you draw from RRSP (the meltdown window) and TFSA. The portfolio works hard early so the guaranteed income floor does the heavy lifting later.

## OAS Deferral vs CPP Deferral: Which First?

If you can only defer one, CPP deferral typically has a larger dollar impact because the base benefit and enhancement rate are both higher. But the strategies complement each other — both increase guaranteed lifetime income, both reduce portfolio dependence, and both provide inflation protection.
