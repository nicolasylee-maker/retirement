Retiring at 55 is one of the most common financial goals in Canada. It's achievable — but it requires a specific plan designed around a 10-year gap before CPP and OAS become available.

Here's a concrete framework.

## The Target Number at 55

Let's build the plan for a single person in Ontario targeting $60,000/year (after tax) at 55.

**Phase 1: Ages 55–65 (No government income)**
Full $60,000/year must come from personal savings.
At $60,000/year for 10 years = $600,000 minimum for the bridge (not accounting for investment returns).

With a 5% real return on investments and ongoing drawdown: approximately $475,000 would fund this phase, drawing down to zero at 65 as government income begins.

**Phase 2: Ages 65–95 (CPP + OAS in payment)**
CPP at 65 (deferred from 60): ~$950/month after reduced contribution years = $11,400/year
OAS at 65: $8,724/year
Total government income: ~$20,124/year
Annual gap: $60,000 – $20,124 = $39,876/year

Savings needed to fund Phase 2 at 4% withdrawal: ~$997,000

**Total target at 55:** Phase 1 bridge + Phase 2 principal = ~$475,000 + $997,000 = **~$1.47 million**

## The Account Structure Matters

The order you draw from accounts determines how much tax you pay and whether income-tested benefits are affected.

**Recommended sequence for a 55-year-old early retiree:**

1. **Ages 55–65:** Draw primarily from TFSA. Zero taxable income. Defer CPP and OAS as long as possible.
2. **Ages 65–71:** Begin OAS. Start selective RRSP meltdown (draw $30,000–$50,000/year from RRSP while income is still relatively low). Top up TFSA with after-tax meltdown proceeds.
3. **Ages 71+:** Convert RRSP to RRIF. By this point, RRSP should be smaller from the meltdown. RRIF minimums are manageable.

## Building to $1.47 Million by 55

For someone starting at 35 with a 20-year runway:
- Required monthly savings (assuming 6% real return, starting from $0): ~$3,500/month
- At a 30% savings rate, this requires income of ~$140,000/year or a household income of ~$200,000+

For someone starting at 40 with a 15-year runway:
- Required monthly savings (6% real return, starting from $0): ~$5,200/month
- This is challenging on average Canadian incomes without inheritance, home equity gain, or professional income

For someone who already has $400,000 saved at 40:
- Additional monthly savings needed: ~$2,800/month over 15 years

## What to Do at 55 Immediately

When you stop working, several things happen simultaneously:

**Employer benefits end.** Source private health and dental coverage immediately — there's often a window for conversion to individual coverage without medical underwriting.

**Group life and disability end.** Disability insurance is irrelevant in retirement, but assess life insurance needs.

**CPP contributions stop.** If you were close to maximizing years, consider whether 2–3 more years of working would meaningfully improve your lifetime CPP.

**TFSA room accumulates.** You continue to gain $7,000/year in TFSA contribution room whether you're working or not.

## The Lifestyle Question

Most retirement planning focuses on whether you *can* retire at 55. Equally important: what will you *do* for the first 10 years?

Early retirees without structure often find the transition harder than expected. Health, social connection, and sense of purpose don't disappear just because you've stopped working. Many early retirees return to work part-time, start businesses, or pursue paid creative work — which also happens to improve the retirement financial plan.

$20,000/year in part-time income from 55–65 changes the required savings number significantly: it reduces the Phase 1 withdrawal by $20,000/year, potentially saving $150,000+ in required capital.
