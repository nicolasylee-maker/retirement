# Reverse Mortgages in Canada: How They Work and Who They're For

You've paid off your home — or mostly paid it off — and it's now worth $700,000, $900,000, or more. But your monthly income is modest and you want more cash flow. A reverse mortgage lets you access that home equity without selling. Here's the full picture.

## What Is a Reverse Mortgage?

A reverse mortgage is a loan secured against your home where you receive money from the lender — either as a lump sum or in instalments — with no requirement to make monthly payments. The loan, plus accumulated interest, is repaid when you sell the home, move out, or die.

In Canada, the primary reverse mortgage product is the **CHIP Reverse Mortgage** from HomeEquity Bank. Equitable Bank also offers reverse mortgages through its program.

## Who Qualifies?

- **Age:** Must be 55 or older (both applicants, if a couple)
- **Property:** Must be your primary Canadian residence
- **Home type:** Single-family homes, condos, and semi-detached (some restrictions apply to condos and rural properties)
- **Equity:** Must have sufficient equity (the loan can't exceed a certain percentage of your home's value)

No minimum income requirement. No credit score check for qualification. Your income and debts don't affect whether you qualify.

## How Much Can You Borrow?

HomeEquity Bank allows you to borrow **up to 55% of your home's current appraised value**. The percentage available depends on:
- Your age (older = more available)
- Your province (Quebec has slightly different limits)
- Your home type and location

**Example:**
- Home value: $800,000
- Age: 70
- Estimated maximum CHIP loan: ~$440,000 (55%)

Most borrowers don't access the maximum. A more common scenario: borrow $200,000 lump sum to supplement retirement income, allowing the remaining equity to continue growing.

## Interest Rates on Reverse Mortgages

This is where reverse mortgages get expensive. In 2025, the CHIP Reverse Mortgage charges approximately **6.5–7.5% per year**, depending on the term and fixed/adjustable choice. This is 1.5–2.5% higher than a conventional mortgage.

Because you're not making payments, the interest compounds. At 7%:
- Year 1: $200,000 borrowed → $214,000 owed
- Year 5: $200,000 → $280,000 owed
- Year 10: $200,000 → $393,000 owed
- Year 15: $200,000 → $551,000 owed

If your home appreciates at 3–4%/year, the equity position may remain stable or even grow. But if appreciation slows and you borrowed a large percentage, the equity cushion erodes.

## Key Protections for Borrowers

Canadian regulations and HomeEquity Bank's terms provide important protections:

**No negative equity guarantee:** You will never owe more than your home is worth. If the home sells for less than the outstanding loan amount, HomeEquity Bank absorbs the difference. Your estate is not responsible for the shortfall.

**No forced repayment while you live there:** As long as you live in the home as your primary residence, maintain it, and pay property taxes and insurance, the bank cannot demand repayment.

**Independent legal advice required:** You must consult an independent lawyer before finalizing a CHIP Reverse Mortgage. This isn't optional — it's a required step.

## Tax Treatment of Reverse Mortgage Proceeds

Reverse mortgage funds received are **not taxable income**. You're borrowing against your asset, not receiving income.

This makes reverse mortgages potentially attractive for retirees near OAS clawback thresholds or those on income-tested benefits like GIS:
- GIS is based on income — reverse mortgage proceeds don't reduce GIS
- OAS clawback triggers on income — reverse mortgage borrowings don't count
- GAINS (Ontario) and other provincial top-ups are similarly unaffected

For retirees who are "house-rich, income-poor," a reverse mortgage can provide cash flow without affecting income-tested benefits.

## Alternatives to a Reverse Mortgage

Before committing, compare these alternatives:

**HELOC (Home Equity Line of Credit):** Lower interest rate (typically 5.5–6.5%), but requires income to qualify and has monthly interest payments. For homeowners who can qualify and afford payments, a HELOC is cheaper.

**Conventional mortgage:** Can refinance to a conventional mortgage to access equity. Requires income qualification and monthly payments.

**Downsizing:** Sell and move to a less expensive home. Frees equity without ongoing interest charges. The principal residence exemption makes the gain tax-free. The "right" answer financially — but emotionally difficult and logistically complex.

**Renting a portion:** If you have extra space, renting a basement suite or room generates income without tapping equity. Rental income is taxable but doesn't require selling or borrowing.

**TFSA and RRSP drawdown:** If you have registered savings, draw them down first and preserve home equity. The order of asset liquidation matters.

## When Does a Reverse Mortgage Make Sense?

**Good fit:**
- You're 70+, plan to stay in your home, and have limited liquid savings
- You need to supplement income without affecting GIS or income-tested benefits
- You want to help children financially ("living inheritance") while staying in your home
- You have high home equity relative to liquid assets
- Your home is in a market likely to appreciate moderately (equity depletion risk is lower)

**Poor fit:**
- You're 55–65 and have many years of interest compounding ahead — the cost becomes substantial
- You plan to move within 5–10 years — setup costs plus accumulated interest make it expensive short-term
- You have sufficient liquid assets and other options
- You have a strong desire to leave the home to heirs — a reverse mortgage significantly reduces or eliminates the estate value of the home

## Costs Beyond Interest

- **Appraisal fee:** ~$300–$500
- **Independent legal advice:** ~$500–$1,500
- **Closing costs:** ~$1,500–$2,000 (title insurance, administration)
- **Penalty fees if you repay early:** Reverse mortgages have prepayment penalties if broken early, similar to conventional mortgages

Total upfront costs: roughly $2,500–$4,500, which are typically deducted from the loan amount.

## The Bottom Line

A reverse mortgage is a legitimate tool for asset-rich, income-poor Canadian homeowners who are committed to staying in their home long-term. The high interest rate is the primary drawback — borrow only what you need, not the maximum available.

For most retirees, downsizing is financially superior. But for those who genuinely cannot or don't want to move, the CHIP Reverse Mortgage provides meaningful cash flow without monthly payments, with the no-negative-equity guarantee providing a safety floor.

Consult a fee-only financial planner before committing — one who has no incentive to sell you either the reverse mortgage or an alternative product.
