The most common retirement question: "Do I have enough?" The honest answer is that it depends entirely on your personal situation — but there are concrete frameworks to work through it.

Here's how to calculate your retirement number for Ontario in 2026.

## Start With Your Annual Spending

The foundation of any retirement calculation is your annual spending. Not your income — your spending. What does it actually cost you to live each year?

Many Canadians are surprised to find retirement spending is lower than expected: no more commuting costs, work clothes, lunches out, or payroll deductions. Studies suggest spending drops 20–30% in the first years of retirement, then stabilizes.

A rough Ontario baseline for a comfortable but not extravagant retirement in 2026:

| Lifestyle | Annual Spending |
|-----------|----------------|
| Modest | $45,000–$55,000 |
| Comfortable | $60,000–$80,000 |
| Generous | $85,000–$120,000 |
| Affluent | $120,000+ |

This assumes a paid-off home. If you're still renting or carrying a mortgage, add those costs.

## The Income Sources You Already Have

Before calculating how much savings you need, subtract the guaranteed income you'll receive regardless:

**CPP:** Average is ~$780/month ($9,360/year). Maximum in 2025 is $1,364/month. Run your My Service Canada account to see your actual estimate.

**OAS:** ~$727/month ($8,724/year) at 65. Can be deferred to 70 for 36% more.

**Employer pension:** If you have a defined benefit pension, this is the biggest factor. Get the exact projection from your plan administrator.

**Example:** A couple in Ontario, both with average CPP and OAS, will receive ~$36,168/year combined from government programs alone — without touching savings.

## The Savings Gap

Your retirement savings only need to cover the **gap** between your spending and your guaranteed income.

**Example:**
- Annual spending: $70,000
- CPP + OAS combined (couple): $36,168
- Annual gap: $33,832

To fund a $33,832/year gap from age 65 to 92 (27 years), at 4% real return, you need approximately **$540,000** in savings.

Use the formula: Savings needed = Annual gap ÷ Safe withdrawal rate

At a 4% withdrawal rate: $33,832 ÷ 0.04 = **$845,800**

At a 3.5% withdrawal rate (more conservative): $33,832 ÷ 0.035 = **$966,600**

The gap between these estimates shows why the withdrawal rate assumption matters.

## Ontario-Specific Tax Considerations

Ontario has some of the highest marginal income tax rates in Canada. In 2026, a retiree with $80,000 in income faces a marginal rate of 43.41% on income above $73,200.

What this means for your savings number:
- RRSP/RRIF withdrawals are fully taxable — a $50,000 RRIF withdrawal costs ~$12,500 in Ontario provincial + federal tax
- TFSA withdrawals are tax-free and don't count as income
- Capital gains in non-registered accounts are taxed at 50% inclusion

**The Ontario retirement advantage:** The Ontario senior tax credit, pension income credit, and age amount reduce effective tax rates for many retirees. A couple each receiving $40,000 in RRIF income pays significantly less per dollar than a single person receiving $80,000.

## The Home Equity Factor

Many Ontario retirees hold significant home equity. The average detached home in the GTA is worth ~$1.1M in 2026. Whether to include this in your retirement plan depends on your situation:

**If you plan to stay:** Home equity is not income. Don't count it unless you plan to downsize or access it via reverse mortgage.

**If you plan to downsize:** The difference between your current home value and your next home (e.g., a condo) can fund 5–20 years of the income gap, depending on the spread.

## The Real Answer

For a single person in Ontario aiming for a comfortable $65,000/year lifestyle:
- Subtract CPP + OAS: ~$18,000/year
- Annual gap: ~$47,000
- Savings needed (4% rule): ~$1,175,000
- Savings needed (3.5% rule): ~$1,343,000

For a couple aiming for $80,000/year:
- Subtract combined CPP + OAS: ~$36,000/year
- Annual gap: ~$44,000
- Savings needed (4% rule): ~$1,100,000

These are starting points. Your actual number shifts based on when you retire, your CPP entitlement, how long you live, and whether you carry any debt into retirement.

## The Most Important Variable: When You Retire

Retiring at 55 vs. 65 doesn't just mean 10 more years of spending. It means 10 fewer years of CPP contributions (lower benefit), 10 fewer years of savings growth, and 10 more years of drawdown. The combined effect can require 40–60% more in savings to retire a decade earlier.

If you're in your 50s and the numbers seem far off, the most powerful lever is usually delaying retirement by 2–3 years, not cutting spending.
