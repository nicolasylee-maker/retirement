# How Rising Interest Rates Affect Your Retirement Plan

After more than a decade of near-zero interest rates, Canada's rate environment shifted dramatically in 2022–2023. The Bank of Canada brought rates from 0.25% to 5%, then eased to around 3% in 2025. This rate cycle has fundamentally changed the retirement planning landscape — in some ways for better, in others for worse.

## The Good News: Safe Income Pays Again

The most direct benefit for retirees and near-retirees: **fixed income actually earns income again**.

In 2021, a 5-year GIC might pay 1.5%. In 2025, the same GIC pays 3.5–4.5%. On a $200,000 GIC portfolio, that's the difference between $3,000/year and $7,000–$9,000/year in interest income — a substantial improvement.

**Practical impact:**
- GIC ladders are viable again as retirement income tools
- Bonds in balanced portfolios contribute meaningful yield
- High-interest savings accounts (HISA) pay 3–5% vs. near-zero
- Money market funds are no longer a "parking place" — they earn returns

For retirees who've avoided risk by holding cash and short-term fixed income, rising rates were straightforwardly positive.

## Annuities Pay More

Annuity rates are directly tied to long-term interest rates. When the Government of Canada 10-year bond yields 3.5%+, insurance companies can afford to pay higher annuity income.

**Comparison:**
- 2021: A 65-year-old investing $300,000 in a life annuity received ~$1,200/month
- 2025: The same $300,000 buys approximately **$1,600–$1,700/month**

That's 33–40% more guaranteed monthly income from the same capital. Retirees who delayed purchasing an annuity during low-rate years and waited for rates to improve were rewarded significantly.

For those considering annuities, current rates (while below the 2023 peak) remain historically attractive compared to the 2010s.

## Bond Portfolios Took a Hit — But Have Recovered

Rising rates are bad for existing bonds. When interest rates rise, bond prices fall — and 2022 was the worst year for bonds in decades.

A long-duration Canadian bond portfolio lost 15–20% in 2022 as rates spiked. This shocked retirees who believed bonds were "safe."

**The mechanism:** If you own a bond paying 1.5% and rates rise to 4%, your bond is worth less (who wants 1.5% when new bonds pay 4%?). The price drops until the yield matches market rates.

However, here's the important context for retirement planning:

1. **If you held through, you recovered.** Bond prices stabilize and eventually recover as you approach maturity.

2. **Short-duration bonds were less affected.** A bond maturing in 2 years barely moved; a 30-year bond fell sharply.

3. **New contributions buy bonds at better prices (higher yields).** The ongoing yield from bond holdings is now much more attractive.

4. **The 60/40 portfolio was challenging in 2022 specifically** because both stocks and bonds fell — an unusual correlation. Historically, bonds cushion equity downturns.

For Canadian retirees, the lesson is to limit bond duration. Short to medium-term bonds (1–7 years) provide most of the diversification benefit with far less interest rate sensitivity.

## Variable-Rate Mortgages in Retirement

Many Canadians in their 50s and 60s have mortgages. If you held a variable-rate mortgage through 2022–2023, your payments may have spiked dramatically.

For retirement planning, this creates an urgent question: **pay down the mortgage aggressively before retiring, or hold investments?**

With mortgage rates at 5–6%, the risk-free return on mortgage paydown is essentially 5–6%. GICs at 3.5–4.5% don't beat a 5–6% mortgage. But equity returns historically average 7–9% — higher than mortgage rates, though with risk.

**Practical guidance:**
- With 5–7+ years to retirement: maintain a balanced approach — don't sacrifice RRSP contributions to aggressively pay the mortgage unless the rate exceeds 6–7%
- Within 3–5 years of retirement: consider aggressively paying down the mortgage for guaranteed "return" and reduced fixed expenses in retirement
- If rates fall significantly before renewal: reassess whether mortgage paydown remains the priority

## Variable vs. Fixed Rates on Renewal

If you're renewing a mortgage in 2025–2026 with potential retirement ahead:

The Bank of Canada has been cutting rates from the 5% peak. Fixed 5-year mortgages run around 4.4–4.8%. Variable rates track the overnight rate.

For someone planning to sell their home in 3–5 years (common before downsizing in retirement), a shorter-term fixed (2–3 year) or even open variable might suit better than a 5-year locked-in rate.

## GIC Laddering Strategy in the Current Rate Environment

With rates having peaked and potentially declining further, there's a strategic case for **locking in current rates now**:

If 5-year GIC rates are currently 4%–4.5% and you expect rates to fall to 2–3% over the next few years, buying 4–5 year GICs today locks in today's rates for years into the future.

This is the classic case for laddering: you don't know exactly where rates are going, so spread maturities across 1, 2, 3, 4, and 5 years. You capture higher long-term rates while maintaining some reinvestment at future rates for short-term rungs.

## Impact on the 4% Rule

The 4% safe withdrawal rule was derived from historical data including periods of varying interest rates. Some financial researchers argue higher interest rates make the 4% rule more conservative (safer), because:
- Bond yields are now higher, improving balanced portfolio returns
- Lower bond sensitivity means less drag on the fixed-income portion

Conversely, higher rates may reflect higher inflation expectations, which erodes purchasing power faster.

In practice: the 4% rule remains a reasonable starting point. If you're concerned about the rate environment, use 3.5% as a more conservative withdrawal rate.

## What to Do Now

**If you're 2–5 years from retirement:**
- Consider locking in some GIC rates now in anticipation of potential rate declines
- Evaluate your mortgage renewal strategy with retirement timing in mind
- If you're planning to annuitize part of your savings, current rates are far more attractive than the 2018–2021 period

**If you're already retired:**
- Maturing short-term GICs may renew at lower rates — build a longer ladder now while rates remain elevated
- Consider whether your asset allocation properly benefits from the improved bond yields
- Revisit annuity quotes — higher rates have meaningfully improved the economics

**For all Canadians:** Higher rates are, on balance, better for conservative savers and retirees than the low-rate era was. Fixed income is no longer a wealth-erosion vehicle in real terms. This improves the sustainability of retirement plans that include significant fixed income.
